Project 2: Tiny Solar Power Supply

You built a whole board of your own in Chapter 8, from an empty project to a folder of manufacturing files, and the workflow walked beside you the entire way. This chapter builds a second board the same way, and it is a gentle step up rather than a leap. The circuit is a little cleverer, most of its parts are surface-mount rather than through-hole, and along the way you will meet a few things the breadboard supply did not need: 3D models, so you can see the finished board in the round before you order it; a closer look at the design rules KiCad hands you for free; and a handful of new moves in the layout — your first via, a ground pour stitched down to it, and cross-probing between the two editors. None of this is new territory in the workflow sense. You still take the eight schematic steps and the eight layout steps in the order you already know, and each one leaves the design ready for the next. Keep the one-page checklist from Chapter 5 beside you, and let’s build a second real thing.

The design is not mine. It is a neat little circuit called the Tiny Solar Supply, drawn by Clemens Valens for Elektor, and I am rebuilding it in KiCad with only a few small changes of my own. Following someone else’s good schematic is exactly how a great deal of real PCB work begins, so it is a fair and useful thing to practise.

Here is what we are building (Figure 9.1): a power supply that runs from a small solar panel. In daylight the panel charges a rechargeable battery; when the panel falls dark, the board draws on that stored charge instead, so the 3.3 volts it delivers never blink out. That makes it a tidy supply for a low-power project left to fend for itself on a windowsill, in a garden, or anywhere a wall socket will not reach.

Figure 9.1: The finished tiny solar power supply, rendered straight from its own design files. The small surface-mount parts sit on top, the three 2-pin headers and the slide switch stand along the edges, and a single board slips into its enclosure on clips. Sun in over one connector, a battery on another, a steady 3.3 volts out, day or night.

The circuit is built from a modest pile of parts:

  • a boost converter chip (U1, an AP3015A), the part that takes a low, wavering input voltage and steps it up to a steady 3.3 volts;
  • an inductor (L1) and two Schottky diodes (D1, D2), the converter’s working partners that store and steer the energy as it is pumped up;
  • four resistors (R1 to R4) and three capacitors (C1, C2, C3) that set the output voltage, steady the rails, and bias the control parts;
  • a small MOSFET (Q1, a 2N7002) that switches the converter on and off automatically, which is the clever heart of the design;
  • three 2-pin headers, for the solar panel (J3), the battery (J2), and the 3.3 volt output (J1);
  • a slide switch (SW1) to cut the whole thing off by hand.

Two of these deserve a word before we start. The first is Q1, the part I called the clever heart. I will come back to exactly what it does when we wire it, because it is the one piece of this circuit that is not obvious. The second is simply that most of this board is surface-mount. On the breadboard supply only the USB-C connector was, and every other part was the friendly through-hole kind. Here the boost converter, the inductor, the diodes, and the transistor are all surface-mount, because that is the only sensible way those particular parts come. I have kept the resistors and capacitors as through-hole so the board stays kind to a hand with a soldering iron, but the surface-mount parts are nothing to fear. They change nothing about how you design the board, and with a little practice they are every bit as manageable as their through-hole counterparts. If soldering the smallest of them still does not appeal, you can ask the fab to place them for you when you order the boards. For now, designing it is all that matters. Let’s gather the parts.

Sourcing the Parts

Before you draw a single wire, gather your parts, exactly as you did in Chapter 8. Run the ‘sourcing decision tree’ from Chapter 6 across the board: for each part, ask in order whether it lives in the standard library, and if not whether a reliable source can give it to you, and only if neither is true do you author it yourself, which still waits for Volume 2. Stop at the first yes.

Here is the full parts list, with the value you will give each part as you place it. Keep it beside you, because the values matter: the pair R1 and R2 in particular sets the output voltage, so they are worth getting exactly right.

Most of this board answers the first question. The four resistors, the three capacitors, the slide switch, and the three 2-pin headers all live in KiCad’s standard library, and you find them the way you have since Chapter 2: press “A,” type a few letters of what the part is, and confirm it in the preview before you place it. The transistor is there too. A 2N7002 sits in the Device library and pairs with a standard SOT-23 footprint, so although it is surface-mount, it needs nothing imported.

Three parts send us to the second question, and they are the three at the heart of the converter: the AP3015A boost chip, the two SS14 Schottky diodes, and the 10 µH inductor. KiCad does not carry these specific parts, so we fetch them from SnapEDA (snapeda.com, now branded SnapMagic), searching by the exact manufacturer part number (AP3015AKTR-G1, SS14, and ASPI-0630LR-100M-T15 respectively) and downloading the KiCad package for each.

SnapEDA is worth a word of explanation, because the choice is not arbitrary. KiCad’s built-in library does carry generic symbols for a Schottky diode or an inductor that might do for a rough prototype, but those generics give you neither the exact footprint for the specific manufacturer part nor a 3D model to check in the viewer. SnapEDA’s part pages show current stock levels and pricing from distributors alongside the download button, so one visit confirms that the part is actually obtainable before you commit it to your design. And each SnapEDA download bundles three things together: a symbol to wire on the schematic, a footprint matched to the real package, and a 3D model, a little STEP file that shows the part’s true shape and height in space. You will not need the 3D model until late in the layout, when we open the 3D Viewer, but it rides along now inside the same download, so keep it with the rest. Save each part’s symbol, footprint, and STEP file together inside the project folder where the project can find them.

The mechanics are the same ones Chapter 6 walked through, so here they are in brief, this time for the AP3015AKTR-G1 boost converter. Type the part number into the search box (❶ in Figure 9.2) and pick the row from the right manufacturer, here Diodes Incorporated (❷); the “Data Available” icons (❸) confirm a symbol, footprint, and 3D model are all on offer. Open that part’s page and click “Download Symbol and Footprint” (Figure 9.3), choose KiCad from the list of CAD formats (Figure 9.4), and pick “V6 & Later” to match a modern install (Figure 9.5). Repeat for the two SS14 diodes and the ASPI-0630LR-100M-T15 inductor.

Do not skip the check Chapter 6 called the most important sentence in the chapter: lay the manufacturer’s recommended land pattern beside the footprint KiCad shows you and confirm the pad count, the pitch, and which pad is pin 1 before you trust it. The test is the same one you know: when you can zip the project folder, hand it to a friend with a fresh KiCad install, and have them open it whole, your parts are sourced. Now let’s draw.

Drawing the Schematic

With the parts gathered, you are ready for the eight-step schematic workflow from Chapter 5. You know every move; I will name each step, give you the details particular to this board, and point you back to Chapter 2 for the mechanics. One thing is different on this project, though: because the board reproduces Clemens Valens’ design, you set the reference designators by hand to match his, rather than letting KiCad auto-annotate. Pinning each part to the designator it carries in the original, so your R1 is his R1, lets you read his schematic straight onto yours, and once every part is named and valued the wiring is a direct trace from his drawing. Here is the schematic you are working toward (Figure 9.7); keep it beside you as your guide.

Figure 9.7: The finished schematic for the tiny solar power supply, after Clemens Valens’ original. This is where the schematic half is headed: every part placed, named, valued, and wired, the three named nets (SOLAR_P, BAT_P, OUT_P) labelled, and plain-text notes beside the connectors and switch (the arrows mark SOLAR, BATTERY, ON/OFF, and OUT) so the sheet reads at a glance.

Step 1, create the project. Make a new project the way you did for the blinker and the breadboard supply, and give it a name without spaces. I am calling mine klp5e-tiny-solar-supply (Figure 9.8). Add your project-local libraries now, so the imported converter, diodes, and inductor are waiting in the chooser when you reach for them: open “Preferences → Manage Symbol Libraries…”, switch to the “Project Specific Libraries” tab, and add the three imported .kicad_sym files with paths built on ${KIPRJMOD}, the substitution that always points at the project’s own folder (Figure 9.9). Do the matching thing for the footprints under “Manage Footprint Libraries…” (Figure 9.10). Then open the Schematic Editor onto its blank sheet.

Step 2, place the symbols. Press “A” and drop a symbol for each part: the converter, the inductor, and the two diodes from your imported library (Figure 9.11), then the four resistors, the three capacitors, the transistor, the slide switch, and the three headers from the standard library. Then press “P” and place your GND power symbols wherever ground belongs. The output rail and the two inputs earn names of their own as well, but those are net labels rather than power symbols, so you will add them when you wire the sheet in Step 5. When every symbol is down, the sheet looks like Figure 9.12: a loose scatter of parts, none yet wired.

The arrows mark the “Add Symbol” tool on the toolbar (the “A” hotkey calls up the same dialog), the selected part, and the OK button that places it.

Figure 9.12: All symbols down, none yet wired. Every part is on the sheet with a question-mark designator and the generic value it was born with (R, C); the next step gives each one its real number and value from the original design.

Step 3, annotate to match the original. Because this board reproduces Clemens Valens’ design, you want your reference designators to match his part for part, so his R1 is your R1 and his schematic reads straight onto yours. That is the one job auto-annotation cannot do: it numbers parts by where they happen to sit on the sheet and pays no heed to anyone else’s scheme. So switch auto-annotation off and give each part the designator it carries in the original: double-click a part, or select it and press “E,” and type the designator into the “Reference” field. The “Annotate Schematic” tool from the “Tools” menu, the one you met on the blinker in Chapter 2, still works (Figure 9.13), but it would number the parts its own way and leave you renumbering by hand to match, so typing them in directly is simpler here. Work through every part (U1, L1, D1, D2, Q1, R1 through R4, C1 through C3, and the headers and switch) until a glance confirms no R? or U? is left.

Figure 9.13: The Annotate Schematic dialog. Left to itself it numbers parts by where they sit on the sheet, which is exactly what you do not want when matching someone else’s design — so here you set the designators by hand and leave this tool alone.

Step 4, set the component values. With each part named, give it its value while the designators are fresh, so the sheet becomes a precise drawing before a single wire is drawn. Double-click a part, or press “E,” and type the value into the “Value” field; or set them all at once in the Symbol Fields Table from the “Tools” menu (Figure 9.14), which lays every part’s value out in a grid and makes it easy to spot one you missed. Take each value straight from the original:

  • R1: 1 MΩ, and R2: 604 kΩ, 1%. The feedback divider that sets the output to 3.3 V; the converter compares their ratio against its internal reference.
  • R3: 10 kΩ, and R4: 1 MΩ. The bias pair that lets Q1 read the panel’s voltage and drive the converter’s shutdown pin.
  • C1: 4.7 µF, 50 V, and C3: 10 pF, 50 V. The small ceramics that steady the input and output.
  • C2: 22 µF, 10 V. The larger reservoir capacitor.
  • L1: 10 µH. The boost inductor, the ASPI-0630LR-100M you imported.

The semiconductors carry their part numbers rather than a plain value: U1 is the AP3015A, D1 and D2 the SS14, and Q1 the 2N7002, each already named by the symbol you placed, so there is nothing to set on those. With every passive valued, the sheet reads as a precise shopping list, ready to wire (Figure 9.15).

Figure 9.14: The Symbol Fields Table after every value is set (the boxed Reference, Qty, and Value columns). Filling the grid in one place makes it easy to confirm no part was left with its generic value before you start wiring.

Figure 9.15: Every part now carries the original design’s reference designator and its value, while the sheet is still unwired. Matching Valens’ numbers and values here makes the last job, wiring, a direct read from his schematic.

Step 5, wire and label. Connect the circuit following Figure 9.7, using the wiring moves from Chapter 2: “W” to draw, “G” to drag a part without breaking its wires, and the small square at a pin vanishing to tell you a join has taken. Because every part is already named and valued, you are simply tracing Valens’ connections onto a finished-looking sheet. Work across the whole circuit; when the last wire is in, the sheet matches Figure 9.16, fully connected with its GND and PWR_FLAG symbols placed, though the three named nets still wait for their labels.

Figure 9.16: The circuit fully wired, with the GND and PWR_FLAG symbols in place. The three named nets, SOLAR_P, BAT_P, and OUT_P, are not labelled yet; that is the next move.

This board is busier than the breadboard supply, and a few wires have to cross one another without joining. KiCad 10 has a tidy new feature that keeps those crossings honest, called ‘hop-over.’ Open “Schematic Setup” from the “File” menu, choose “Formatting,” and under “Connections” set “Hop-over size” to “Small” (Figure 9.17). From now on, wherever one wire passes over another without connecting, KiCad draws a little hop in the upper wire, the way a footbridge steps over a path. A crossing with a hop is two separate nets; a crossing with a solid junction dot is a join. With the hops switched on you can read a dense sheet without second-guessing a single intersection.

Figure 9.17: Turning on ‘hop-over’ in “Schematic Setup,” under “Formatting.” With “Hop-over size” set to “Small,” a wire that crosses another without joining shows a small hop, so a crossing is never mistaken for a connection.

Three nets earn names, so give them labels with the “L” hotkey, which opens the Label Properties box: pick the label tool (❶ in Figure 9.18), type the net’s name such as OUT_P (❷), and click OK (❸) to drop it on the wire. Name SOLAR_P for the panel input at J3, BAT_P for the battery at J2, and OUT_P for the regulated output that feeds J1. Everything else ties together by being wired directly or by sharing a GND symbol, so you need not drag long wires across the sheet. Two more small badges go on now: place a PWR_FLAG where power enters the board and a second PWR_FLAG on the GND net, pressing “P” and searching for PWR_FLAG just as you would for any power symbol. What these two do, and why the design needs them, is the subject of the very next section; for now, place them and move on.

Figure 9.18: Creating a net label. The label tool (❶) opens the Label Properties box; type the net’s name (❷), here OUT_P, and click OK (❸) to attach it to the wire. A label joins every wire that carries the same name, wherever it sits on the sheet, so naming a net is the same as wiring all its parts together.

Before you wire Q1, let me explain it, because it is the one part of this circuit a beginner would never guess at and the reason the supply is more than a plain booster (Figure 9.19). The job is subtle: in bright daylight, when the panel has plenty to give, the battery should simply charge and the converter should rest, so it wastes nothing. When the panel falls dark, the converter should wake up and boost the battery’s charge up to 3.3 volts so the output keeps flowing. Q1, with the bias resistors R3 and R4, is what makes that decision by itself. It senses the panel’s voltage and drives the converter’s shutdown pin: while the sun is up it holds the converter off, and as the panel fades it lets the converter run. Diode D2 plays its own quiet part here, blocking current from leaking back out of the battery into the panel at night. You wire it once and the board manages day and night on its own, with no microcontroller and no firmware. The output voltage itself is set by the divider of R1 and R2, whose ratio the converter compares against its internal reference to settle on very nearly 3.3 volts.

Figure 9.19: The clever heart of the board. Q1 reads the panel’s voltage through R3 and R4 and switches the converter off in daylight and on in the dark, with no firmware at all. Note too the small hop where one wire crosses another without joining, the hop-over you turned on a moment ago.

First, drop a few plain-text notes beside the connectors and switch with the text tool (“T”): SOLAR at J3, BATTERY at J2, ON/OFF at SW1, and OUT at J1. These are tied to no net and change nothing electrically, but they say at a glance what each port is for, as you saw on the finished sheet in Figure 9.7. Second, fill in the sheet’s title block. Open “Page Settings” from the “File” menu and set the title, a revision, and a line or two of comment (Figure 9.21); it is only right to credit the original here, so mine reads “Designed by Clemens Valens for Elektor,” with a note that it is used with permission. KiCad prints what you type into the corner of the sheet (Figure 9.22), so the schematic carries its own provenance wherever it travels.

Finding and Fixing a Design Defect

Run the Electrical Rules Check the way you learned in Chapter 2: open it from the “Inspect” menu and click “Run ERC.” This time the report comes back clean, with zero errors (Figure 9.26). That is not luck. It is the two PWR_FLAG badges you placed while wiring, doing their quiet job. A PWR_FLAG tells the ERC, on your honour, that real power enters the design at the net it sits on. Power reaches this board through connectors, the solar panel at J3 and the battery at J2, and a connector’s pins look passive to KiCad, so without a little help the checker cannot tell that power truly arrives there. The flag on the incoming supply is that help, and a second flag sits on the GND net.

Figure 9.26: With both PWR_FLAGs in place, the Electrical Rules Check comes back clean: zero violations, with only the handful of tests KiCad ignores by default set aside.

A clean report is the goal, but it is worth understanding why those flags are needed, because that understanding is the part you carry to the next board. The surest way to understand a check is to watch it fail, so let us break the design on purpose. Find the PWR_FLAG on the GND net, delete it, and run the ERC again. Now the checker complains (Figure 9.27): “Input Power pin not driven by any Output Power pins,” with a marker dropped on the ground net.

Figure 9.27: Remove the GND flag and the complaint appears: “Input Power pin not driven by any Output Power pins” (boxed in the report), with a violation marker dropped on the GND symbol in the schematic behind it (the arrow).

Why should deleting one small badge cause that? The answer is in how KiCad classifies every pin. Double-click the converter, U1, to open its symbol properties, and choose the “Pin Functions” tab (Figure 9.28). Each pin has an “Electrical Type,” and pin 2, GND, is set to “Power input.” The single pin on every GND power symbol is a power input too. KiCad’s rule is simple: any net carrying power-input pins must also carry something that promises power is genuinely delivered to it, either a pin set to “Power output,” or a PWR_FLAG standing in for one. While the GND flag was in place, it satisfied that rule. With it gone, the GND net is full of power inputs and nothing vouches that power reaches them, so the checker stops and points.

Figure 9.28: The root of the complaint. In U1’s “Pin Functions” tab, the GND pin (pin 2) is typed “Power input” (boxed), and a net full of power inputs needs something to vouch that power truly arrives.

The fix is to put the badge back. Place a PWR_FLAG on the GND net again, press “P” and search for PWR_FLAG as before, then run the ERC once more. The error clears and the report is clean again, exactly as it was in Figure 9.26.

Notice what you never had to flag. The OUT_P rail carries no power-input pins clamouring to be driven, so the checker never asks you to vouch for it: the converter produces that rail, and the passive parts around it simply draw on it. A PWR_FLAG belongs only where power enters through something the checker sees as passive, a connector or a battery, or on a shared net like GND that has no driving source of its own. It never belongs on a rail an actual chip is producing. This is the same skill Chapter 8 first named: read every line the checker shows you rather than chase an empty report blindly. Knowing which complaints are real, and which would only be noise, is most of what makes the ERC worth running.

With the schematic drawn, named, footprinted, and genuinely clean, complete the eighth and last schematic step: save once more, then choose “Update PCB from Schematic” from the “Tools” menu to carry the whole design across the one-way bridge into the PCB Editor. Time to make it physical.

Laying Out the Board

You are on the eight-step layout workflow from Chapter 5 now, and you know every move from the breadboard supply. I will name each step, flag what is special about a mostly surface-mount board, and weave in the new moves this project brings. Here is the board you are heading for (Figure 9.29).

Figure 9.29: The finished layout. Small and two layers, with the surface-mount parts grouped on the top copper, the connectors and switch along the edges, and a solid ground pour on the bottom that a single via stitches the converter’s ground pad down to.

Step 1, import and look at the design rules. If the handoff worked, the PCB Editor already holds your parts in a clump laced with the ratsnest (Figure 9.30); if not, press “F8” to bring them across.

Figure 9.30: The parts arrive from the schematic in a single clump, every pad tied to its neighbours by the thin lines of the ratsnest. Untangling that clump into a board is what the rest of this chapter is about.

This project promised a closer look at the design rules, so open “Board Setup” from the “File” menu and find them now, under “Design Rules → Constraints” (Figure 9.31). The ‘design rules’ are the safety limits KiCad measures your board against: the smallest gap allowed between two pieces of copper, the narrowest a track may be, the size of a via’s hole, how close copper may come to the board’s edge. The defaults are deliberately conservative, comfortably inside what any ordinary fab can make, and for a small, low-current board like this one they need no change at all. Two numbers here are worth a glance, because you will meet them again before the chapter is out: the copper-to-edge clearance, set to 0.5 mm, which is the rule the design rules check will catch us breaking later; and the minimum thermal relief spoke count, set to 2, which has a hand in how firmly the ground pour grips each pad. You would tighten any of these only when a board grew dense enough to need finer copper or carried enough current to need wider, and that judgement waits for a later volume. For now, read them once so they are not a mystery, and leave them as they are.

Figure 9.31: The design rules, under “Design Rules → Constraints” in Board Setup. These conservative defaults — minimum clearances, track width, via and drill sizes, the 0.5 mm copper-to-edge spacing, and the thermal relief spoke count — are the limits every later check measures the board against. On a board this small you read them once and leave them alone.

While Board Setup is open, glance at the stackup too, because a two-layer board is right here as it was before. Under “Board Stackup → Physical Stackup” you can see the two copper layers, F.Cu on top and B.Cu on the bottom, with a 1.6 mm FR4 core between them (Figure 9.32). That bottom layer is where the ground pour will go.

Figure 9.32: The physical stackup of a plain two-layer board: a copper layer on each face of a 1.6 mm FR4 core. The top carries the parts and their signal tracks; the bottom will hold a solid ground pour.

Step 2, the board outline. This project has no dimensions handed to it from outside, so the goal is simply to make the board as small as the parts allow. Sketch a rough rectangle on Edge.Cuts to work inside — the same move from Chapter 3, on a 0.5 mm grid so the edges land on round millimetres (Figure 9.33) — then place the parts, and come back at the end to pull the outline in tight around them. I drew mine generously at first and shrank it once everything had a home.

Figure 9.33: A first rough rectangle on Edge.Cuts, drawn on a 0.5 mm grid. It is only a working boundary; once the parts are placed you pull it in to make the board as small as it can be.

One decision belongs here, before you settle on a size: how the finished board will be held in its box. On the breadboard supply you met the screw-down ‘mounting hole,’ and you could add four of them at the corners here too. But a small board like this one does not have to be bolted down. It can drop into a moulded enclosure and be held by clips, or by a pair of slots in the box — no holes, no screws, and a little more board freed up for parts. That is the route I have taken, so this board carries no mounting holes at all; the outline simply has to fit the enclosure’s inside. It is worth knowing that not every board needs mounting holes, and that the choice is best made early, because it shapes the outline you draw.

Step 3, place the components. A few parts have to meet the outside world in set places, so start with them: put J3 (solar) and J2 (battery) along the left edge, J1 (output) at the right edge, and the slide switch SW1 toward the middle where a thumb can reach it. Then arrange the rest between them.

This is the first board busy enough that it pays to be sure, as you nudge a part, exactly which part you are holding. KiCad has a quiet feature for that called ‘cross-probing,’ on by default (you will find its switch under “Preferences → Schematic Editor → Display Options → Cross-probing”). Put the schematic and the layout side by side, and the two editors talk to each other: click a part in one and its twin lights up, centred, in the other (Figure 9.34). Click C2 on the schematic and C2’s footprint jumps into view on the board; click a footprint you cannot name on the board and the schematic shows you which part it is and what it does. On a sheet of near-identical resistors and capacitors that is a real relief, and it keeps you placing the part you mean to place.

Figure 9.34: Cross-probing between the two editors. With the schematic (left) and the layout (right) side by side, clicking C2 in one centres C2 in the other (the arrows), so you always know which part you are working with.

A word on how to arrange the parts, since the breadboard supply did not ask for much thought here and this board does. There is no single right answer, but a few habits serve you well. Let the board echo the schematic: signals tend to flow left to right on a well-drawn sheet, so I kept the inputs on the left — the solar panel J3 and the battery J2 — and the output J1 on the right, with the converter doing its work in between. Inside that frame, place by connection rather than by tidiness: keep each capacitor hard against the part it steadies, gather the converter with its inductor and its diodes so the busy loop between them stays short, and let the ratsnest be your guide — every time a part finds a better home the thin lines pull shorter and cross less. Then temper all of that with a thought for the hand that will build the board, and leave enough air between the surface-mount pads that a soldering iron can reach one without melting its neighbour. Short tracks and a buildable board usually want the same thing, so the two goals rarely fight. Nudge until the ratsnest is short and untangled and every part can be reached, and the placement is done (Figure 9.35).

Figure 9.35: The placement settled. The inputs J2 and J3 sit at the left edge and the output J1 at the right, the switch SW1 falls in the middle, and the surface-mount parts are grouped between them with room to solder — the ratsnest pulled short and untangled throughout.

Step 4, route the tracks. Route as you did on the breadboard supply: press “X,” set your layer and width, and turn each ratsnest line into copper. Keep every signal track on the top copper, where the surface-mount parts live. This board has room enough that no track needs to dive to the other side to slip past another, so you will not reach for a via in this step at all. (One via does appear on the finished board, but it belongs to the ground pour in step 5, and I will come to it there.)

With room to spare there is no need to juggle widths either: set a single track width of 0.5 mm and draw every line at it. Why 0.5 mm, though, and not some other number? The width you give a track is governed by the current it must carry: wider copper carries more current, with less heating and less voltage drop along the way. The currents here are small — the whole board runs on what a solar panel and a little battery can give — so even a much thinner track would carry them safely; 0.5 mm simply buys comfortable margin and keeps the drop low on the power path, where a supply repays you with efficiency. It is also well inside what any fab can make, so manufacturing sets no limit either. One width, no fuss.

Rather than retype that width on every track, set it up once where KiCad keeps such things. Open “Board Setup → Design Rules → Pre-defined Sizes” and add the track widths you mean to use — I listed 0.25 mm, 0.35 mm, and 0.5 mm — and, in the same dialog, the via size you will want in the next step: a 0.5 mm pad on a 0.3 mm drill (Figure 9.36). Once they are defined they wait for you in the dropdowns on the toolbar, so you pick a width or a via from a short menu instead of typing a number and trusting that you typed the same one as last time.

Figure 9.36: Pre-defined track widths and via sizes, set once under “Board Setup → Design Rules → Pre-defined Sizes.” With 0.25, 0.35, and 0.5 mm tracks and a 0.5 mm / 0.3 mm via defined here, each is a click away on the toolbar while you route, with no retyping.

There is a tidier way still to handle track width when a board really needs it, worth knowing even though we skip it here. KiCad can set widths automatically by the role a track plays, through a feature called ‘net classes’: you sort the nets into groups, the power nets into one class with a wide track and the signal nets into another with a thin one, and KiCad then draws each track at the width its class dictates, with no choosing as you go. On a board this simple that is overkill, so we route at a single width and move on; but it is a clean, scalable habit, and we will put net classes to work on the next project, in Volume 2.

With one width chosen and every signal track laid on the top copper, the front of the board fills in (Figure 9.37). Keep going until the status line reads “Unrouted: 0” — but leave the ground connections alone for now; we will handle every ground at once with a pour in the next step.

Figure 9.37: The top copper fully routed at 0.5 mm. Every signal track lies on the front of the board; the ground connections are left deliberately bare for the pour that comes next.

Step 5, add a copper fill, and stitch the ground down. Pour a ground plane on the back copper, just as in Chapter 3: make B.Cu active, draw a filled zone over the whole board, and in the Copper Zone Properties that open, set the zone’s net to GND (Figure 9.38). Press “B” and KiCad floods every empty corner of the bottom layer with copper tied to ground.

Figure 9.38: The Copper Zone Properties for the ground pour: net GND, on B.Cu. The ‘thermal relief’ settings lower down — the gap and the spoke width — govern how the pour reaches each pad it touches, which matters more than it looks; we come to it below.

Here is the wrinkle a surface-mount board adds, and it is worth understanding because you will meet it on every board like this. Your through-hole ground pads — the GND pins on the headers, on the switch, on the resistors and capacitors — pass right through the board and touch the pour on the bottom directly, so the flood ties them in for free. But the converter U1 is surface-mount: it sits on the top layer, and its ground pad has no leg reaching through to the bottom, so the pour cannot find it on its own. The two need joining, and this is where the board earns its single via.

A ‘via’ is a plated hole that carries a connection from one copper layer to another — a tiny lift shaft for a signal, dropping it from the top of the board to the bottom. This is the first one we have placed. Rather than route a ground track all the way across the top copper to find the pour’s edge, click U1’s ground pad, draw a very short stub off it, and while you are still routing press “V” to drop a via on the end of the stub (the 0.5 mm pad on a 0.3 mm drill you predefined a moment ago). The via bores straight down to the pour on the bottom layer and carries the pad’s ground with it. That is the whole move: one short segment and one via, and the converter is grounded, with no long top-side detour to clutter the board (Figure 9.39). It is a humble trick, but it is exactly how ground is reached on dense, high pin-count boards, where dropping straight down to a ground plane through a via is the normal — and often the only practical — way in.

Figure 9.39: The converter’s ground stitch, up close. Pad 2 (GND) runs a short stub of top copper to a single via — the gold ring with its drill at the centre — which carries the connection straight down to the ground pour on the bottom layer. The converter’s other pads keep their own signal tracks on top.

Now back to those thermal reliefs, because the pour does not simply weld itself solid to every pad it surrounds. Look closely at a grounded pad after the fill and you will see the copper reach it through a few narrow spokes, with a thin moat of clearance around it otherwise (Figure 9.40). That is a ‘thermal relief,’ and it is there for the soldering iron: a pad welded straight to a large copper plane sheds heat so fast that solder can be hard to make flow, so KiCad deliberately necks the connection down to a handful of spokes that still carry the current but no longer drain the iron’s heat. The number of spokes matters. Too few — one, or two on opposite sides — and the joint is mechanically and electrically weaker, and KiCad will warn you if it drops below the minimum spoke count you saw set to two back in the design rules. The trap is the board’s edge: where a ground pad sits too close to the outline, the pour cannot wrap all the way around it and a spoke or two is lost. So I kept the GND pads a little clear of the edges, enough that each one keeps at least three spokes. You can steer this with two handles — the thermal relief gap and spoke width in the zone’s properties (Figure 9.38), and the minimum spoke count in the design rules — but the simplest lever on a small board is just to give the grounded pads room to breathe away from the edge.

Figure 9.40: The ground pour filled on the bottom copper. Each grounded pad connects to the plane through a thermal relief — the few narrow spokes that bridge the clearance moat — and the pads were kept clear of the board edge so every relief keeps at least three spokes.

Step 6, the design rules check. Run the DRC from the “Inspect” menu, leaving “Refill all zones before performing DRC” ticked so the pour is fresh when it checks, and “Test for parity between PCB and schematic” ticked so nothing has drifted from the schematic. Click any violation to jump straight to it on the board, fix it, and run again — the same loop as before.

On this board the check caught one real error, and it is a common one: a board-edge clearance violation, “Pad 1 of L1” too close to the edge (Figure 9.41). The design rules ask for at least 0.5 mm of breathing room between any copper and the board’s outline — the number you read in step one — and the inductor’s first pad had crept inside it. There were two honest fixes: nudge the bottom edge of the board down a millimetre or two to make room, or move L1 up and away from the edge. There was ample space above, so I moved the part rather than grow the board, and the re-run came back clean (Figure 9.42).

Figure 9.41: The Design Rules Checker reporting one error — “Board edge clearance violation… Pad 1 of L1” — with the offending pad picked out at the board’s edge (the arrow). Clicking the line jumps to the pad so you can see exactly what the rule is unhappy about.

One caution when you fix a clearance error by moving a part: the part is already routed, so its tracks, and any via on its pads, move with it, and a track that was straight can end up stretched, kinked, or pulled tight against a neighbour. After you nudge a routed part, look at the segments that start and end on its pads and redraw any that the move has left awkward — a clean short track is worth the half-minute it takes to lay again.

Figure 9.42: After moving L1 clear of the edge and re-running, the checker reports zero violations. The two remaining warnings are harmless silkscreen overlaps, which we tidy in the next step.

Read each remaining line, clear the real ones, and confirm in passing that the converter’s ground via took — an unconnected GND pad here would mean the single stitch from step five never quite landed. You may set aside the harmless silkscreen-overlap warnings for the next step.

Step 7, add text and silkscreen. Most of your silkscreen rode across as reference designators. Add the board’s name and version by hand on F.Silkscreen; I am labelling mine solar psu v2. As on the breadboard supply, print the polarity beside each header, + and −, so a future you cannot wire the panel, the battery, or the output backwards, and a short on/off by the switch and a word at each port — solar, bat, OUT — do the same kindness. On the back, it is only right to credit the original: I add a line reading “Tiny Solar Power Supply, after Clemens Valens for Elektor.” Nudge any label the DRC grumbled about clear of the pads as you go, and the front of the board reads cleanly (Figure 9.43).

Figure 9.43: The silkscreen finished. The board’s name sits on the copper, + and − mark the polarity at each header, and solar, bat, on/off, and OUT name the ports — so the board explains itself to whoever builds or wires it.

Step 8, dress the board in 3D, and generate the Gerbers. The chapter promised 3D models, and here is where they pay off. Open the 3D Viewer from the “View” menu and KiCad renders the board as a solid object, every part standing at its true height above the copper. It is the quickest sanity check there is: a part facing the wrong way, a footprint that does not match its real shape, or two tall parts about to collide all leap out here in a way they never do on the flat layout.

But the render is only as good as the models behind it, and this is the part that takes a little setting up. A ‘3D model’ is a small STEP file describing a part’s real shape; KiCad pairs one with a footprint so the viewer knows what to draw. The imported parts you fetched from SnapEDA already carry theirs — the converter, the inductor, and the diodes each brought a STEP file along inside the download, so they appear correctly with no further work. The trouble is the everyday parts. KiCad’s own library of 3D models is large, and a default install does not always keep every model on disk, so a resistor or a pin header can come up as a bare footprint with nothing to show.

The fix is to fetch the official model library once and point KiCad at it. The models live in the KiCad project on GitLab, at gitlab.com/kicad/libraries/kicad-packages3D; open that page, use the green “Code” button, and download the whole library as a zip (Figure 9.44). Unzip it somewhere permanent on your disk — I keep mine under a KiCad_shared folder — and the generic models become available to assign.

Figure 9.44: The KiCad 3D model library on GitLab. Open gitlab.com/kicad/libraries/kicad-packages3D (❶), use the “Code” button (❷), and download the library as a zip (❸). Unzip it somewhere permanent, and KiCad’s generic models — resistors, capacitors, pin headers — are there to render in the viewer.

With the library in place, attaching a model to a part is a short ritual. Double-click a footprint to open its Footprint Properties, choose the “3D Models” tab, and use the folder button to browse for a STEP file; pick the one that matches — here the right-angle pin header for J2 — and the preview shows it before you commit with OK (Figure 9.45).

Figure 9.45: Adding a 3D model to a footprint. In Footprint Properties → “3D Models,” browse the unzipped library (❶, ❷) for the matching STEP file and confirm with OK (❸). The preview on the right shows the model before you commit it.

A model rarely lands perfectly aligned the first time. The same tab gives you Scale, Rotation, and Offset boxes to nudge the model until it sits squarely on its pads — a right-angle header that imports facing the wrong way, for instance, may want a half-turn about the Z axis and a small offset to seat it (Figure 9.46). And once you have one part configured you need not browse the file system again for its twins: copy the model’s full path from the configured part and paste it into the next, so the three identical headers, or all four resistors, take the same model in seconds.

Figure 9.46: The Scale, Rotation, and Offset boxes (❶) align a model with its pads; here the right-angle header is turned 180° about Z and offset to seat on its footprint. Copy the full model path (❷) from one part and paste it into the next that uses the same model, and you skip the file browser entirely.

With every part modelled, the viewer shows the finished board whole (Figure 9.47): the converter with its inductor and diodes at their true heights, the through-hole headers and the slide switch standing along the edges, the tall electrolytic C2 beside them.

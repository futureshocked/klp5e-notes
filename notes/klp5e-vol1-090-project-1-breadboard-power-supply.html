Project 1: Breadboard Power Supply

You have done the whole thing once, and then some. In Chapter 2 you drew a schematic, in Chapter 3 you turned it into a manufacturable board, and you did both with my hand on yours at every click. Chapter 5 pinned those two walks down as a checklist you can reuse forever, and Chapter 6 gave you the one skill both tours quietly leaned on: finding and importing any part a design needs. This chapter is where it all comes together. You are going to build a complete board of your own, from an empty project to a folder of manufacturing files, following the same fifteen steps you already know. The difference is that this time the board is yours to drive. I will still walk beside you, but where the tours told you which button to press, here I will more often point back to the chapter where you learned the move and let you make it.

This is your first solo board, so let me hand you the safety net before you need it: the workflow. As long as you take the eight schematic steps and the eight layout steps in order, each one leaves the design in a state the next one expects, and you will not get lost. Keep the one-page checklist from Chapter 5 open beside you, and treat this chapter as the project that proves you can follow it without me reading it out.

Here is what we are building (Figure 8.1): a small power supply for your breadboard. It takes 5 volts in over a ‘USB Type-C’ connector, the same reversible USB connector you met in Chapter 6, and delivers that 5 volts to your breadboard’s power rail through a two-pin header and a couple of jumper wires, with a switch to turn it on and off, a fuse to keep a stray strand of wire on the breadboard from harming your computer, and an LED that glows when the power is flowing.

Figure 8.1: The finished breadboard power supply. This is where we are headed: 5 volts in over USB-C, clean 5 volts out to the breadboard.

You might wonder why a power supply has no regulator chip in it, the way many do. The answer is that USB already hands us a clean, regulated 5 volts, which is exactly the voltage a breadboard full of 5-volt parts wants. So this board’s job is not to make 5 volts but to deliver it: to bring it in safely, switch it, protect it, show that it is live, and present it where jumper wires can reach. That makes it an honest, genuinely useful first project, and a gentle step up from the blinker rather than a leap.

The circuit is built from a handful of parts:

  • a USB Type-C receptacle (J1), where the USB cable plugs in;
  • two 5.1 kΩ resistors (R1, R2), the small but essential pair that tells a USB-C source to switch its 5 volts on (more on these in a moment);
  • a slide switch (SW1) to turn the output on and off;
  • a resettable fuse (F1) that trips if the breadboard draws too much, then heals itself once you clear the fault;
  • two capacitors (C1, C2) that steady the supply;
  • an LED (D1) and its resistor (R3) as a power-on indicator;
  • a 2-pin header (J2) that feeds the breadboard’s power rail.

How the Board Works

Before you draw it, it is worth a few minutes to understand what each part is for, because a board you understand is a board you can fix. The whole design has one job, and the intro already named it: this board does not make 5 volts, it delivers them. So the clearest way to read it is to follow the power from where it enters to where it leaves, along the same spine you will wire as the schematic (Figure 8.2).

The path is short. Five volts arrives on the receptacle’s VBUS pin once the USB-C source agrees to provide it. From there it passes through the fuse F1, which breaks it for you if something goes wrong, and then through the slide switch SW1, which simply lets you break the path by hand. Past the switch it becomes the +5V rail, and everything that draws power hangs off that rail: the output header J2 that feeds your breadboard, the indicator LED through its resistor, and the two capacitors. Ground is the return road for all of it, running from every part back to the connector’s ground pins. The two resistors on the CC1 and CC2 pins are the one part that does not sit on this path; they are how the board asks the source to turn the 5 volts on in the first place, and because that is the single trickiest idea in the chapter, I have saved its full explanation for the moment you wire it.

The fuse deserves a closer look, because it is not the glass-and-wire kind you may be picturing. F1 is a ‘resettable fuse,’ also sold as a ‘polyfuse’ or a ‘PTC,’ where the letters stand for ‘Positive Temperature Coefficient,’ which is just a careful way of saying its resistance climbs as it heats up. In normal use it is barely warm and barely there, a few tenths of an ohm the current hardly notices. But if a stray wire on your breadboard shorts the rail to ground, the sudden rush of current heats the little polymer body fast, its resistance leaps by thousands of times, and the current collapses to a harmless trickle within a fraction of a second. Clear the short and it cools, its resistance falls, and the fuse heals itself, ready for next time. That is the gift it gives you, and the thing it is really protecting is the USB port on your laptop or charger, which would far rather see this part trip than feed a dead short.

The two capacitors are a pair on purpose, because they do different jobs. C2 is a larger electrolytic, and it works as a small reservoir: when something on your breadboard suddenly asks for more current, C2 has charge stored and ready to hand over for the instant it takes the supply to catch up, so the rail does not sag. C1 is a small ceramic, and it handles the fast, fidgety noise a reservoir is too slow to mop up, shorting those high-frequency wiggles quietly to ground right where they appear. A useful way to picture the pair is a water tank and a shock absorber on the same line: one steadies the big, slow changes, the other soaks up the quick jolts. Nearly every board you build will carry this same pair for the same reason.

Finally, the indicator. D1 is an ordinary LED, and R3 is the resistor in series with it, and that resistor is not optional. An LED is not a resistor: connect one straight across 5 volts and it will try to pass far more current than it can survive, and it will die, usually in a brief, bright flash. R3 is there to hold the current down to a safe, comfortable level. You size it with the same Ohm’s-law sum every indicator uses: take the 5 volts, subtract the roughly 2 volts the LED drops while it is lit, and divide what is left by the current you want, where a few milliamps is plenty to see. That points you at a resistor of around a kilohm, and the exact value is not fussy, because all you are asking the LED to do is glow brightly enough to tell you the power is on.

One last promise before we start. Somewhere in the middle of this build, the Electrical Rules Check is going to stop you with a complaint that looks alarming and is, in fact, the single most common stumble on a first board that takes its power from a connector. We will meet it head-on, understand exactly what it is telling us, and clear it in a way you will reuse on every powered board you ever draw. Let’s build something real.

Sourcing the Parts

Before you draw a single wire, gather your parts. Remember the ‘sourcing decision tree’ from Chapter 6: for every part you ask the same three questions in order, and you stop at the first yes. Is it in the standard library? Then use it. If not, can a reliable source give it to you? Then import it. Only if no reliable source has it do you author it yourself, and that, as you know, waits for Volume 2. Let’s run the tree across this board’s parts and have everything ready before we start drawing.

Most of what we need answers the first question with a comfortable yes. The resistors, the capacitors, the LED, the slide switch, the fuse, and the 2-pin header all live in KiCad’s standard library, and you find them the same way you found the blinker’s parts in Chapter 2: press “A,” type a few letters of what the part is, and confirm it in the preview pane before you place it. The generic building blocks (resistors, capacitors, the LED) sit in the Device library, the switch and the fuse in libraries of their own, and the header among the Connector libraries. None of these will give you any trouble.

One part sends us to the second question, and it is the one you have already met. The USB Type-C receptacle is exactly the connector we walked through importing in Chapter 6. Two details are worth pinning down before you fetch it. First, you want a receptacle, the socket a cable plugs into, and not a plug, which is the cable’s own end; and you want the power-only kind, because it brings out the two separate CC pins this board leans on while skipping the data pins it has no use for. Second, because the connector has to sit at the board’s edge with its opening facing outward, as you will place it in the layout, choose a horizontal, board-edge receptacle rather than a vertical one whose opening would point at the ceiling. The part I settled on is a GCT USB4125-GF-A-0190: a power-only, horizontal Type-C receptacle whose six signal contacts solder to the surface, but whose four corner mounting legs, its ‘stakes,’ drop through the board. I chose the through-hole-staked version on purpose. A USB connector is tugged every time a cable goes in or out, and stakes that anchor through the board shrug off that strain far better than a surface-mount-only part, whose pads can lift and peel. The ruggedness costs nothing and pays off on a board you will plug into for years.

Here is a twist worth dwelling on, because it sharpens what it really means to source a part. This receptacle is common enough that KiCad actually ships a footprint for it, so you could place it straight from the standard library. We are going to fetch it from SnapMagic anyway, and the reason is the lesson. A part sitting in the library tells you only that someone drew a footprint for it; it says nothing about whether you can still buy one. The unhappy version of this story is the designer who builds a whole board around a tidy library part, sends it off, and only then learns the component was discontinued, leaving a scramble for a substitute whose footprint never quite fits. Looking the part up on SnapMagic closes that gap in a single step: the page shows it in stock and links me to distributors, so I confirm at once that the part is real, buyable today, and backed by the manufacturer’s own model. So we do exactly what Chapter 6 taught: search SnapMagic for the part number, download the KiCad package with the symbol, footprint, and 3D model together, and add it to the project. And here you get a small bonus the next part may not hand you: because KiCad carries this footprint too, you can check the one you imported against the standard one as well as against the datasheet.

Do not skip the check that Chapter 6 called the most important sentence in the chapter: lay the manufacturer’s recommended land pattern beside the footprint KiCad now shows you, and confirm the pad count, the pitch, and which pad is pin 1 before you trust it. A connector that is right in every way except a mirrored pin assignment is a miserable fault to chase on a finished board, and a one-minute look now prevents it. This matters a touch more here than it did for an ordinary chip, because the receptacle’s signal pads are the only surface-mount joints on this board, and a surface-mount pad leaves you no room to fudge a misplaced pin with a bent leg.

That surface-mount detail deserves a word of reassurance. Every other part on the board is through-hole, the friendly kind you met in Chapter 1, and the connector is the only one with surface-mount joints, though even it sinks those through-hole stakes into the board to hold itself steady. That changes nothing about how you design the board; once the footprint is in your library it behaves like any other. And if hand-soldering its small surface-mount contacts does not appeal, you can ask the fab to assemble that one part for you when you order the boards. For now, designing it is all that matters.

Finally, do the housekeeping that keeps a project healthy: copy the parts you are using into a project-local library, exactly as Chapter 6’s last section described, so the design carries its own copies and still opens cleanly years from now. The imported connector especially belongs inside the project, reached through the project-specific library table with a path built on ${KIPRJMOD}. The test, you will remember, is simple: when you can zip the project folder, send it to a friend with a fresh KiCad install, and have them open it whole, your parts are sourced. Now let’s draw.

Drawing the Schematic

With the parts gathered, you are ready for the eight-step schematic workflow from Chapter 5. You know all eight moves; I will name each step, give you the few details particular to this board, and point you back to Chapter 2 for the mechanics. On this board I take two of them in a slightly different order, wiring before I set the component values: with every part annotated as it is placed, the canonical steps 4 and 5 swap freely, and wiring first lets one figure show the bare circuit and the next the same sheet once its values are filled in. Here is the schematic you are working toward (Figure 8.2); keep it beside you as your guide, the way you used the blinker’s schematic in Chapter 2.

Figure 8.2: The finished schematic for the breadboard power supply. This is where the schematic half is headed.

Step 1, create the project. Make a new project the way you did for the blinker, and give it a name without spaces. I am calling mine bb-psu, for ‘breadboard power supply.’ Open the Schematic Editor onto its blank sheet. If you have not already, add your project-local libraries to this project’s library tables now, so the imported connector is waiting in the chooser when you reach for it.

Steps 2 and 3, place and annotate the symbols. Press “A” and drop a symbol for each part: the USB-C receptacle from your imported library, the two resistors for the connector, the LED and its resistor, the two capacitors, the slide switch, the fuse, and the 2-pin output header. Then press “P” and place your power symbols. Here we make one small, deliberate choice. The blinker called its positive rail VCC, a generic name, because its supply could be anything from 3 to 9 volts. We know exactly what ours is, so name the positive rail +5V instead; KiCad has a power symbol of that name. Place a +5V flag and a GND flag wherever the supply and ground belong. Naming the rail for what it actually is makes the schematic easier to read and your intent easier to check.

As each part lands it needs its reference designator, the R1, C1, or J1 that names it for the rest of the project. Recent versions of KiCad hand these out for you: with auto-annotation switched on, every part takes the next free number the moment you drop it, the question marks never appear, and there is nothing more to do. It is a real time-saver, and it is how I work on a board like this. You can also annotate by hand, and there is one case where you will reach for it: when you are copying a reference design and want your designators to match it part for part, so that your R7 is their R7. Auto-annotation numbers parts by where they happen to sit on the sheet and pays no heed to anyone else’s scheme, so for that job you switch it off and type each designator into the part’s properties yourself, or fall back on the “Annotate Schematic” tool from the “Tools” menu that you met on the blinker in Chapter 2. Whichever route you take, make sure every part shows a real designator, with no R? or J? left behind. The difference is easy to see: Figure 8.3 shows the parts just as they land, every designator still a question mark, and Figure 8.4 shows the same sheet once annotation has given each one a real number.

Figure 8.3: All ten symbols placed on the sheet, each still wearing the question-mark designator (R?, C?, J?) it arrives with.

Figure 8.4: The same symbols after annotation — every designator now a real number (R1, C1, J1), with no question marks left.

Before you wire anything, let me explain the pair of resistors I keep mentioning, because they are the one part of this circuit that is not obvious and the one beginners leave out. A USB-C source, a laptop port or a charger, does not simply push 5 volts down the cable to whatever plugs in. It waits to be asked. The asking happens through two pins on the connector called CC1 and CC2, and a device that wants power asks by tying each of those pins to ground through a 5.1 kΩ resistor (Figure 8.5). Those resistors, R1 and R2, are how your board says “I am here, and I would like the 5 volts now.” Leave them out and a proper USB-C charger gives you nothing at all, and you will spend an evening wondering why a board with no visible fault is stone dead. Put them in, and the supply comes alive the moment you plug in. This is the kind of detail that never came up on the blinker and shows up the instant you connect real hardware to the modern world.

Figure 8.5: The two resistors that make USB-C work. Each CC pin pulls to ground through 5.1 kΩ, which is how the board asks the source for its 5 volts.

Step 5, wire and label. We come to the wiring before the values, the small reordering flagged above. Connect the circuit following Figure 8.2, using the wiring moves from Chapter 2: “W” to draw, “G” to drag a part without breaking its wires, and the small square at each pin vanishing to tell you a join has taken. The path of the power is the spine of this board: it comes in on the receptacle’s VBUS pin, passes through the fuse and then the slide switch, and arrives at the +5V rail that feeds the output header, the indicator LED through R3, and the steadying capacitors. Ground returns from the header and every part back to the connector’s ground pins. Tie CC1 and CC2 each to ground through their 5.1 kΩ resistor as we just discussed. Just as the blinker’s GND and VCC symbols connected by name without a wire crossing the sheet, your +5V and GND symbols tie the rails together wherever you drop them, so you need not drag a single long wire across the drawing for power or ground. When the last wire is in, your sheet matches Figure 8.6: fully connected along the power spine, though most parts still wear the generic values they were born with.

Figure 8.6: The schematic fully wired. The CC resistors already show their 5.1k value, but R3 and the two capacitors still read the generic R and C they were born with — setting those is the next step.

Step 4, set the component values. This is the step the canonical order places just before wiring; on this board we have just done the wiring, so we arrive at it now. Your parts were annotated as you placed them, back in steps 2 and 3, so the job that really remains before footprints is giving every part its value. Most parts still wear the generic value they were born with, a resistor that just reads R, a capacitor that just reads C. Now you tell each one what it actually is, so the schematic reads as a precise shopping list. Double-click a part, or select it and press “E,” to open its properties, and type the value into the “Value” field. Set them as follows:

  • R1, R2: 5.1k. The two CC resistors, and the most important values on the whole board. Get them right and the USB-C source switches its 5 volts on; leave them generic and you will build a board that looks perfect and sits stone dead, the very trap described earlier.
  • R3: 1k. The LED’s series resistor. As “How the Board Works” explained, anything near a kilohm is fine here; it simply holds the LED current to a comfortable few milliamps.
  • C1: 100nF. The small ceramic that shorts the fast, fidgety noise to ground.
  • C2: 10uF. The electrolytic reservoir. Anything from about 10 to 100 µF does the job; a larger value just stores a little more charge.
  • F1: 500mA. The resettable fuse’s hold current: it carries half an amp without complaint and trips at roughly twice that, enough to protect your computer’s USB port while leaving a breadboard plenty of headroom.

The remaining parts, the receptacle J1, the slide switch SW1, the LED D1, and the output header J2, have no value to set in this sense; their designator and chosen footprint say all the board needs to know. With the resistors, capacitors, and fuse valued, every part on the sheet now declares exactly what it is. If you would rather set them all in one place, the Symbol Fields Table from the “Tools” menu lays every part’s value out in a grid (Figure 8.7), which also makes it easy to spot one you missed. Either way, the finished drawing now reads as a precise shopping list (Figure 8.8).

Figure 8.7: The Symbol Fields Table (from the “Tools” menu) lists every part’s value in one grid — a quick way to set them all at once and confirm none was left generic.

The receptacle, J1, is the exception, and the reason you did the sourcing work first: assign its footprint either from the KiCad standard library or from the library you imported — the two are equivalent, so use whichever you prefer, confirming it against the datasheet as you did when sourcing. That leaves step 7, the Electrical Rules Check, and step 8, the handoff to the board. Save your work with “Ctrl+S,” because we are about to run the check, and the check is where this chapter earns its title.

Finding and Fixing a Design Defect

Run the Electrical Rules Check the way you learned in Chapter 2: open it from the “Inspect” menu, choose “Electrical Rules Check,” and click “Run ERC.” On the blinker, a clean schematic gave you zero violations and you moved straight on. This time the report comes back with a complaint (Figure 8.10), reading something like “Input Power pin not driven by any Output Power pin.” In fact it fires three times over, once for each power net on the board: ❶ VBUS, where the supply comes in; ❷ +5V, the rail past the fuse and switch; and ❸ GND, the return. Each numbered marker in Figure 8.10 is the small arrow KiCad drops on the net it cannot confirm is driven.

Figure 8.10: The ERC stops on a power net it cannot confirm is driven. This is the most common stumble on a first connector-powered board.

Take a breath, because nothing is actually wrong with your circuit. The wiring is correct, the parts are right, and the board you have drawn would work. Remember that back in Chapter 5, when I listed the kinds of slip the ERC catches, one of them was “a power input with nothing driving it.” This is that check firing, and here is what it really means. KiCad gives every pin an electrical character. The pins that carry your supply, the receptacle’s VBUS pin and the loads on the +5V rail, are marked as power inputs: pins that consume power. The ERC has a sensible rule that a net full of power inputs ought to have at least one power output feeding it, something that says “real power genuinely arrives here.” On this board the only thing feeding your +5V rail is a connector, and a connector’s pins, as far as KiCad knows, just sit there passively. So the ERC cannot find anything that promises the rail is truly powered, and it stops to ask you a fair question: are you sure power gets in here?

You answer that question with a special symbol called PWR_FLAG. A PWR_FLAG is not a component; it is a little badge you attach to a net to tell the ERC, on your honour, “power enters the design at this point, I promise.” Place one on each of the three power nets the checker flagged — VBUS, +5V, and GND. You add it like any power symbol: press “P,” search for PWR_FLAG, and drop one onto each of those nets, usually right at the connector where the power really does come in. Figure 8.11 shows all three in place: ❶ on VBUS, ❷ on +5V, and ❸ on GND. Then run the ERC again, and the three violations are gone.

Figure 8.11: A PWR_FLAG on each of the three power nets tells the ERC that power genuinely enters here. With all three placed, the report comes back clean.

Two things about the PWR_FLAG are worth keeping. The first is that it is a note to the checker, not a part on the board. It has no footprint, carries no current, and when you hand the design across to the PCB in a moment, nothing physical comes with it. You will not find a PWR_FLAG anywhere on your finished board, and that is exactly right. The second is the habit it teaches. Almost every board you ever design that takes its power from a connector, a battery holder, or a barrel jack will need a PWR_FLAG for the same reason, because a source of power that KiCad sees as passive needs you to vouch for it. Now you know the move, and the next time the ERC stops you with this exact complaint, you will recognise an old friend rather than a fault.

This is also a good moment to sharpen the skill Chapter 2 first named: understanding every line the checker shows you, rather than chasing an empty report at any cost. The deleted-wire error you made on purpose in Chapter 2 was a real fault, a connection genuinely missing. This one was not a fault at all; it was the checker asking you to confirm your intent, and the fix was to confirm it. A good designer can tell the two apart, and telling them apart is most of what makes the ERC valuable. A clean report you do not understand is worth less than a report with one violation you have read, understood, and judged.

With the schematic drawn, named, footprinted, and now genuinely clean, complete the eighth and last schematic step: save once more, then choose “Update PCB from Schematic” from the “Tools” menu to carry the whole design across the one-way bridge into the PCB Editor. Every part arrives knowing its footprint, and, as promised, no PWR_FLAG among them. Time to make it physical.

Laying Out the Board

You are on the eight-step layout workflow from Chapter 5 now, and once again you know every move from the blinker in Chapter 3. I will name each step, flag what is special about laying out a power board, and trust you with the mechanics. This board, though, has something the blinker did not: it has to fit a real piece of hardware, the breadboard it will power, and that breadboard decides its shape.

So look at the breadboard before you draw the board. Figure 8.12 shows the one this supply is made for. A power rail runs down each long side, a row of holes marked + beside a row marked −, the two rows a tidy 2.54 mm apart, and feeding those rails is your board’s whole job at this end. Rather than perch the supply on top of the breadboard, where it would bury the very holes you want to build on, you will stand it flush alongside one end and carry the power across in short jumper wires. Its output is a plain two-pin header, J2: two short jumpers take +5V and GND from it into the first + and − holes of the near rail, and a second pair carries them across to the rail on the far side, so the whole breadboard comes alive from one small connector while every working hole stays clear.

Figure 8.12: The breadboard this supply is built for. The + and − rails on each side sit 2.54 mm apart (marked). The supply stands beside the breadboard and feeds the near rail with short jumper wires from its two-pin output header; another pair feeds the rail on the far side.

That settles how the board meets the breadboard, and it points straight at a shape: a tall, narrow rectangle that stands on its own beside one end of the breadboard, as tall as the breadboard is wide so a header at the top can reach the near rail and, later, one at the bottom can reach the far rail. Standing it alongside rather than on top keeps the USB-C cable out in clear air and leaves every hole on the breadboard open. Before you open the editor, sketch it. Five minutes with a pencil, deciding which part lives at which edge and how the power threads from the connector to the output, saves a long session of shoving parts around the canvas wondering where they belong. Figure 8.13 is exactly that kind of plan: the board drawn on paper, every major part given a home before a single footprint is placed.

Figure 8.13: Plan the layout on paper first. The board stands beside the breadboard’s left end: the USB-C receptacle J1 takes the left edge, opening out; the two-pin output header J2 sits at the top-right corner, pins pointing up, a short jumper-hop from the first holes of the top power rail; the slide switch SW1 and the indicator LED D1 go where a thumb and an eye can reach them; and the power runs in a spine from the connector through the fuse and switch to J2. A second pair of jumpers carries +5V and GND to the far rail, with room at the bottom-right for a future second header, J3.

Treat this sketch as a starting point, not a contract. Its job is to settle the big decisions — which part lives at which edge, and how the power threads from the connector to the output — before you wrestle with the canvas, and those rarely change. The finer placement almost always does. Once you are in the PCB Editor with the real footprints and the ratsnest in front of you, you will nudge parts to shorten a track or clear a courtyard, and the board will drift a little from the pencil drawing. On this one, for instance, I expect the real footprints to pack tighter than the pencil plan reserves room for, so once they are placed I will pull any unused board edge back in — trimming the height, and later the width — to end up with a smaller, cheaper board than the full-size sketch implies. The sketch gets you to a plan you trust; KiCad is where you tighten it.

Figure 8.17: The parts arrive clumped together and laced with the ratsnest — exactly what a fresh import looks like, just as on the blinker.

Step 2, the board outline. Switch the active layer to Edge.Cuts (❶ and ❷ in Figure 8.18) and draw a rectangle that comfortably surrounds the parts, the same way you did in Chapter 3. Switch to a 1 mm grid first so the edges land on round millimetres; KiCad shows the rectangle’s width and height as you drag (❸ and ❹), so you can read the size off straight away. Let the sketch from Figure 8.13 set it: make it about 55 mm tall, the same as the breadboard is wide from the top rail to the bottom one, so the header at the top reaches the near rail and a future header at the bottom could reach the far rail; the width only needs to fit the parts, so keep it narrow and the board takes little room beside the breadboard. You can always drag an edge later — wider if the parts feel cramped, or in to shed space they do not use, as we will do in a moment.

Figure 8.18: Draw the board outline on Edge.Cuts. Set the layer (❶) and pick it in the Layers panel (❷); the width and height readouts (❸, ❹) let you size the rectangle as you drag — a tall, narrow shape here, on a 1 mm grid.

Step 3, place the components. This is where a power board asks for a little more thought than the blinker did, because two of its parts have to meet the outside world in particular places, and you already settled both when you sketched Figure 8.13.

Begin with the output header J2, because the breadboard fixes where it must sit. Switch to a 0.254 mm grid now for finer positioning (❷ in Figure 8.19); it lines up with the pitch of the breadboard’s power rails, so J2 can land exactly where a pair of jumpers will reach. Put it at the top-right corner with its pins pointing up, so a pair of female jumper wires can drop onto it and run the short hop into the first + and − holes of the breadboard’s top rail. Two moves are worth meeting here, because both earn their keep on every board from now on. The first is the ruler, KiCad’s measuring tool: pick it from the right-hand toolbar and drag it across a gap to read the distance without changing anything on the board (❸), a quick way to confirm a clearance or a hop is what you think it is. The second is locking: once J2 is exactly where you want it, right-click it and choose “Lock,” and a later stray drag can no longer nudge your one carefully placed part out of position. A small padlock then marks it as fixed.

Figure 8.19: Place the output header J2 at the top-right corner on the 0.254 mm grid (❷). The ruler tool measures a distance without altering the board (❸) — here confirming the hop to the rail — and J2 is then locked so it cannot be nudged later.

The USB-C receptacle must sit right at the edge of the board with its opening facing outward, so a cable can actually plug into it; a connector stranded in the middle of the board is useless. Place J1 hard against the left edge, opening pointing out, lining its “PCB Edge” mark up with the board’s edge (Figure 8.20). Leave this one unlocked for now, since you may still nudge it as the rest of the board falls into place.

Figure 8.20: The receptacle J1 placed against the left edge with its opening facing out, its “PCB Edge” mark aligned to the board edge. J2 is already fixed at the top-right; the rest of the parts wait in a clump, still laced by the ratsnest.

Then arrange the rest as Chapter 3 taught, watching the ratsnest go short and untangled as you nudge parts, and keeping courtyards from overlapping. Put the switch where your thumb can reach it and the LED where you will see it. Add a mounting hole or two near the corners if you would like to bolt the board down, the same MountingHole_3.2mm_M3 you used before.

When every part is placed, step back and look at the board as a whole (Figure 8.21). On my first pass the parts packed into the top of the board and left a stretch of empty copper below them — the “wasted space” the figure marks. The sketch had reserved that lower end for a second header, J3, to feed the breadboard’s far rail directly, but the parts plainly did not need the room, and an emptier board is a bigger, costlier board for nothing. So I dragged the bottom edge up to meet the parts, ending with the shorter, tidier board in Figure 8.22. This contradicts the full-height sketch on purpose, and the trade is yours to make either way: keep the full height if you want to fit that second header later, or trim it as I did for the smallest, cheapest board. I prefer the efficient use of space, so the rest of this chapter continues with the trimmed board, and feeding the far rail stays an exercise — a second pair of jumpers from J2 carries +5V and GND across just as well.

Figure 8.21: All parts placed, but at the board’s full height the lower third is empty — the “wasted space” marked here. The sketch had kept that room for a future header; the real parts did not need it.

Figure 8.22: The same board after dragging the bottom edge up to meet the parts. A smaller, cheaper board, at the cost of the bottom-rail header — a trade you can decline by keeping the full height instead.

Step 4, route the tracks. Route as you did on the blinker — press “X,” set your layer and track width, and turn each ratsnest line into copper, dropping a via with “V” where two tracks need to cross on a two-layer board — but this board adds one wrinkle worth a moment’s setup. The blinker used a single 0.4 mm width for everything, and that is fine for the small currents a breadboard draws. Here, though, the path that carries all the current, from the connector through the fuse and the switch to the +5V rail and back along ground, is worth drawing a little wider than the signal tracks, say 0.8 mm. It costs nothing, and a fatter power track is simply sturdier. To route with two widths, define them both up front so they are waiting in the track-width dropdown. Open “File” then “Board Setup,” go to “Design Rules” then “Pre-defined Sizes,” and add 0.4 mm and 0.8 mm as track widths (Figure 8.23). They now appear in the dropdown above the canvas, ready to pick as you route.

Figure 8.23: Define the track widths you will use before you route. In “Board Setup” under “Design Rules” then “Pre-defined Sizes,” add 0.4 mm and 0.8 mm; both then appear in the track-width dropdown above the canvas.

With the widths defined, route the power spine first. Pick 0.8 mm from the dropdown (❶ in Figure 8.24) and, on F.Cu (❷), draw the wide path from the connector through the fuse and switch to the +5V rail and back along ground. Then switch the dropdown back to 0.4 mm and route everything else (Figure 8.25), dropping a via with “V” wherever two tracks must cross. Keep going until the status line at the bottom-left reads “Unrouted: 0.”

Figure 8.24: Route the power spine first, wide. With 0.8 mm picked in the dropdown (❶) and F.Cu active (❷), the current-carrying path is drawn as the fat copper run.

Figure 8.25: Switch back to 0.4 mm and route the remaining signal tracks. The narrower copper fills in around the wide power spine until nothing is left unrouted.

Step 5, add copper fills. Pour a ground plane on the back copper, just as in Chapter 3. Make B.Cu active and draw a filled zone around the board; when you close the shape, the Copper Zone Properties dialog opens, where you set its net to GND (Figure 8.26). At first the zone is just an outline (Figure 8.27); press “B” and KiCad floods it with copper, tying every GND pad into the plane through the small spoke-shaped “thermal reliefs” that keep those joints easy to solder (Figure 8.28). A ground pour is good practice on nearly every board, and a power supply is no exception; it gives every grounded part a short, solid path back to the connector.

Figure 8.26: Start the pour on the back copper. With B.Cu active (❶), draw the zone, then set its net to GND in the Copper Zone Properties dialog (❸) and click OK (❹).

Figure 8.27: Straight after you draw it, the zone is only a boundary — its filled area is still zero. Nothing is poured until you fill it.

Figure 8.28: Press “B” and the zone floods with copper. Each GND pad ties into the plane through thermal-relief spokes — the J2 pad here joins on two spokes, another on four — and the corners are softened by the zone’s chamfer setting.

A pour is not frozen once it is made. Double-click it to reopen its properties and change its geometry like any other object — here I tightened the clearance, set a wider thermal-spoke width, and changed the corner smoothing (Figure 8.29). That is enough to alter how each pad ties in: after the change, the J2 GND pad joins the plane on three spokes instead of two. The one thing KiCad will not do is refill the zone for you, so keep the habit from Chapter 3: press “B” after any change to see the new fill before you check or export (Figure 8.30).

Figure 8.29: A pour can be edited after creation. Double-click it to reopen the Copper Zone Properties dialog and adjust the clearance and minimum width, the thermal-relief gap and spoke width, and the corner smoothing radius (boxed).

Figure 8.30: After editing, press “B” again to refill. The new fill hugs the board more tightly, and the J2 pad now ties in on three spokes — proof that the change took, and a reminder that a pour only updates when you ask it to.

Step 6, the design rules check. Run the DRC from the “Inspect” menu, leaving “Refill all zones before performing DRC” ticked so your pour is fresh when it checks. Click any violation to jump straight to it, fix it, and run again until the report is clean, exactly the loop you ran on the blinker. On my board the first run flagged two “unconnected items” — the receptacle’s metal shield, or mounting “stake,” pads were not yet tied into the GND net (Figure 8.31). That is a real fault, not a false alarm: those pads belong on ground, and it is an easy one to miss because the ratsnest lines hide under the pour. The fix is to route them, a short GND track from each shield pad into the plane (Figure 8.32). Run the check again and it comes back clean — zero violations and zero unconnected items (Figure 8.33). The DRC studies physical reality: copper too close to copper, a pad too near the edge, a courtyard overlap, a connection still missing. The surface-mount receptacle gives it a little more to look at than the all-through-hole blinker did, so read each line, fix it, and re-run until it comes back clean.

Figure 8.31: The first DRC run finds two unconnected items: the receptacle’s shield/stake pads (SH) are not yet joined to the GND net. The leaders point from the report to the offending pads on J1.

Figure 8.32: The fix is to route the missing connections — a short GND track from each shield pad into the surrounding pour, drawn here at 0.8 mm on F.Cu.

Figure 8.33: Re-run the DRC and the report is clean: zero violations, zero unconnected items. The board is now physically correct.

Step 7, add text and silkscreen. Most of your silkscreen is already there, carried across as reference designators. Those designators often land larger than a small board wants, and you can restyle them all in one pass rather than one at a time: select the board, open “Edit” then “Edit Text and Graphic Properties,” scope the change to “Reference designators,” and set a single text width and height for the lot (Figure 8.34). Then add the board’s name and version by hand on F.Silkscreen, the way you signed the blinker; I am labelling mine BB-PSU v1.0. On this board there is one extra label worth printing, because a future you will be grateful for it: mark which output pin is +5V and which is GND, right beside the header. A power connector with no polarity printed on it is an invitation to wire your breadboard backwards, and a few letters of silkscreen prevent it. With the labels placed and sized, the silkscreen reads cleanly (Figure 8.35).

Figure 8.34: Restyle every reference designator at once with “Edit Text and Graphic Properties.” Scope the change to reference designators (❶), set one text width and height (❷), choose bold if you like (❸), then “Apply and Close” (❹).

Figure 8.35: The finished silkscreen: tidy reference designators, the board name and version BB-PSU v1.0, the +5V and GND labels beside J2, and ON/OFF beside the switch.

Step 8, generate the Gerbers. Before you export, open the “3D Viewer” from the “View” menu for the free sanity check Chapter 3 recommended. On this board it earns its keep twice over: check that the USB-C receptacle sits flush at the board edge with its opening clear (Figure 8.36), then flip the board over to confirm the GND pour covers the back as you expect (Figure 8.37). A part facing the wrong way is far cheaper to catch here than on real copper. One thing you will notice is missing from the render: the parts themselves. The viewer is showing the bare board and the flat footprint outlines because I did not attach 3-D models to these components. KiCad can also stand a full, realistic model of every part up on the board for a complete virtual prototype — the board and its components together — and you will add exactly that in the next project.

Figure 8.36: The 3D Viewer is your last sanity check. Confirm the connector sits flush at the edge before you spend a cent. The parts are drawn only as flat outlines here because no 3-D models were attached.

Figure 8.37: The back of the board in the 3D Viewer, showing the GND pour filling the bottom copper around the pads.

The 3D view showed me two small things worth tidying before I committed the board, so I took a short detour — neither is essential, but both are cheap and easy. The first is cosmetic: square corners are sharp, so I rounded them. Select the four Edge.Cuts lines, right-click, and choose “Shape Modification” then “Fillet Lines” (Figure 8.38), then enter a radius — 2 mm here (Figure 8.39). The second is alignment: the board edge sat a hair wide of the USB-C receptacle, so I pulled that edge in to meet the connector, rather than move the connector itself, which would have dragged its traces along with it. The result is the rounded, trimmed outline you already saw in Figure 8.14, and re-checking it in the 3D Viewer confirms the connector now sits right at the edge (Figure 8.40).

Figure 8.38: To round the board corners, select the Edge.Cuts lines, right-click, and choose “Shape Modification” then “Fillet Lines.”

Figure 8.39: Enter the fillet radius — 2 mm here — and KiCad replaces each sharp corner with a smooth curve.

Figure 8.40: The finished board back in the 3D Viewer after rounding the corners and trimming the width so the edge meets the USB-C receptacle.

When it looks right, plot the Gerber files from the “File” then “Plot” dialog, choosing the layers your fab needs (Edge.Cuts among them) and a folder to hold the output (Figure 8.41). Then click “Generate Drill Files” and write the drill file into the same folder (Figure 8.42), exactly as in Chapter 3. Your output folder now holds a board waiting to be made.

Take a moment with what you have just done. With no one telling you which button to press, you sourced every part for a real board, imported and verified the one the standard library did not have, drew and checked a complete schematic, met and understood the most common stumble in all of KiCad, and laid out a finished, manufacturable two-layer board. That is not a tour any more. That is you, designing.

Going Deeper: Reference Designs and Further Reading

This board is deliberately the simplest honest version of its idea, stripped to what a first project needs and no more. If it has whetted your appetite, here are three places I would send you to see the same idea drawn by other hands: the first if you want a fuller design to study, the next two if you want to really understand the USB-C trick at its heart. None of them is required reading. They are here for the curious.

The BrødBoost-C2, by Axiometa, is the closest cousin to our board, and it is open-source hardware with the full KiCad files published, so you can open the real schematic and layout and poke around inside them. It is built on exactly the foundation we used: a USB-C receptacle, the two 5.1 kΩ CC resistors, a resettable fuse of about an amp, an on/off switch, and a power LED. Where it goes further is the instructive part, because it adds the things we chose to leave off a first board: a small on-board regulator so it can also offer 3.3 volts, protection against static on the USB lines, and a reverse-polarity ‘ideal diode’ that shrugs off a backwards connection. Reading it is the best way to see what a more grown-up version of the board in your hands would add, and why. You will find it at https://hackaday.io/project/205468-axiometa-brdboost-c2-breadboard-power-supply, with the design files at https://github.com/axiometa/axiometa_hardware.

Tyler Ward’s “Using USB Type-C on hobbyist projects” is the article I would hand anyone who wants to understand the two resistors. It is the piece much of the maker world first learned this from, and its minimal example is, near enough, our J1, R1, and R2 and nothing else. It explains plainly why each CC pin needs its own 5.1 kΩ resistor to ground, and what a source will and will not offer a board that asks this way. Read it at https://www.scorpia.co.uk/2016/03/17/using-usb-type-c-on-hobyist-projects/.

Hackaday’s “All About USB-C: Resistors and Emarkers” is the friendly deep dive. It walks through the same CC-resistor system we used and, more usefully, the ways it goes wrong, which is exactly the “stone dead board” trap I warned you about. It also explains why a board with a receptacle uses two resistors where a captive cable uses only one, and what lies beyond plain 5 volts if you ever want a board to negotiate for more. Find it at https://hackaday.com/2023/01/04/all-about-usb-c-resistors-and-emarkers/.

It is a gentle step up rather than a leap: you will meet 3D models and mounting holes properly, and source a part or two from outside the standard library again, with the same fifteen-step spine carrying you the whole way. For now, save your work, keep your Gerbers safe, and let’s go build the next one.

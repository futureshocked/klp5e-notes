KiCad Like a Pro, 5th Edition — Volume 1

Introduction

Welcome to KiCad Like a Pro. I have been designing PCBs with KiCad since version 4, and this book contains nearly everything I have learned along the way. Whether you are picking up a PCB design tool for the first time, or arriving from a different CAD application, my aim is to get you productive as quickly as possible.

What You Will Learn

Volume 1 is written for readers with little or no experience in PCB design. No prior knowledge of KiCad is assumed either. By the time you finish this volume, you will have taken two real circuits from blank schematic sheets to manufactured, physical boards.

The first project is a breadboard power supply: a compact board that takes a standard 9 V wall adapter and delivers regulated 5 V and 3.3 V rails to a breadboard. It is a useful thing to own, and it is a good first board. A handful of components, no fine-pitch parts, a straightforward layout that fits in the palm of your hand.

The second project is a small solar power supply. It adds a little more complexity in both the schematic and the layout, and gives you practice with the choices you face every time you sit down to design a new board.

More important than the boards themselves are the two workflows they introduce. You will learn an 8-step schematic design workflow and an 8-step layout design workflow. I use these same workflows for every PCB I design. Once they are familiar, you can tackle any new project with a clear sequence of steps rather than a blank stare at the screen. The workflows are the durable part of Volume 1. The projects put them into practice.

The book is roughly half concepts and half hands-on work. The first five chapters lay the foundation: what a PCB is, how to read a schematic, how to read a layout, the key design concepts, and the two workflows themselves. The next four chapters walk through the projects step by step. Chapter 10 covers getting your finished board manufactured, and Chapter 11 is a recipe collection for tasks you will return to again and again.

How This Book Is Organised

If you are new to PCB design, read the chapters in order. Each chapter builds on the ones before it, and jumping ahead will leave gaps that become problems once the projects start.

If you already have experience with PCB design but are new to KiCad, you can move more quickly. Chapter 7 gives you a tour of the KiCad editors. From there you can jump to the projects, using the earlier concept chapters as a reference whenever you need specific background.

At the back of the book you will find the ‘Mini Encyclopedia.’ This is a reference section, not reading material. It covers PCB terms and KiCad techniques in depth, and I point you to it from the project chapters whenever a topic deserves more space than the main text allows. If you are curious about a particular term, the Mini Encyclopedia is the first place to look.

All of the screenshots in this book were captured using KiCad 10, the version covered throughout. KiCad looks and behaves essentially the same on Windows, Mac, and Linux, so the images should match what you see regardless of your platform.

What You Will Need

To follow along with the projects, you will need:

  • A computer running Windows, Mac OS, or Linux.
  • An internet connection, for downloading KiCad and searching for component data.
  • A mouse with at least two buttons and a scroll wheel. KiCad’s canvas relies on the scroll wheel for zoom; a trackpad is workable but less comfortable over a long session.
  • The ability to install software on your computer.
  • Time, and patience.

That is all. No special hardware, no test equipment, no PCB mill. The design work stays entirely on your computer through the end of Chapter 9.

Why KiCad?

Why do I use KiCad? Because it is the perfect PCB software for my use.

I am an electrical engineer with a background in electronics and computer engineering, but above all I am a technology educator and hobbyist. Most of my PCB projects eventually find their way into my books and courses. My projects are modest in complexity, much like the kind of thing a serious hobbyist or solo developer would build. For that kind of work, KiCad proved to be exactly the right tool.

Your situation may be different. You may be a university student completing an engineering degree, a hobbyist building something for fun, a solo developer putting together a prototype, or part of a team working on a commercial product. KiCad fits all of these well. Here is why I chose it, and why I keep choosing it.

KiCad is free and open-source. The source code is available for anyone to download, inspect, and build. While I am not rigid about open-source versus commercial software, when a genuinely excellent open-source option exists, I take it. KiCad is one of those cases. Being free matters especially if you are a student or hobbyist. Professional-grade PCB software can run to hundreds of dollars a year on a subscription, and for most of us, those tools offer nothing that KiCad does not.

KiCad is also unlimited. There is no “standard” tier, no “professional” tier, and no ceiling on board size or layer count that you must pay to lift. You download one installer and you get everything. Other tools offer free licenses for hobbyists or students, but they always come with restrictions on board dimensions, on what you can do with the output, or on where you are allowed to manufacture. There is always the risk that the vendor changes the terms. KiCad carries none of those risks. It is unlimited and it is yours forever. I feel strongly enough about this that I make a yearly donation to CERN to support KiCad’s continued development.

The feature set is professional grade. Interactive routing, differential pair routing, length matching, multi-sheet schematics, a configurable design rules checker, and a built-in SPICE simulator are all part of a standard installation. You probably will not need all of these in Volume 1, but they are there when you do. KiCad can also be extended through a plugin system and through Python scripting, so if there is a workflow it does not support out of the box, you can add it.

The community behind KiCad is large and active. Hundreds of thousands of people worldwide use and contribute to the project. Contributing organizations include CERN, the Raspberry Pi Foundation, Arduino LLC, and Digikey Electronics. Translations cover more than a dozen languages. PCB manufacturers have taken notice: many now publish KiCad-specific ordering guides, and some accept the native KiCad project file directly, in addition to the industry-standard ‘Gerber’ format. Component libraries compatible with KiCad are available on major repositories such as SnapEDA and Octopart.

KiCad runs on Windows, Mac, and Linux, and your project files are fully portable across all three platforms. I use it on all three myself and have never had a problem moving a project between machines. Your finished board files can be sent to any fabricator that accepts Gerber files, which covers every professional fabrication service I have ever used.

KiCad’s development now follows a yearly release cycle, which means you can count on a predictable schedule of improvements. Each new major version has been more polished and capable than the last. KiCad 10, which this book is based on, is the most fully featured version yet.

For all of these reasons, KiCad is the tool I reach for every time I sit down to design a PCB, and it is the tool I have chosen to teach with in this book.

If you are coming from another CAD application, I ask one thing: an open mind. KiCad looks and behaves differently from other tools. The easiest way to learn it is to set aside your expectations and approach it the way a first-time user would. As the Borg famously warned, “resistance is futile.” In learning, as in so many other things, you are much better off going with the flow.

The Concepts Behind What You Did

You’ve now done something a lot of people never get around to: you drew a complete schematic in one editor, then turned it into a real board layout in another, all the way to files a factory could build. You did it by following along, and that was the point. In these last two chapters I asked you to act first and understand later.

Now let’s close the loop. This short chapter explains the why behind the actions you just performed. None of it is busywork: the handful of ideas here are exactly what turn KiCad from a set of buttons you follow into a tool you understand. Once these click, everything later in the book, and every project you ever design, rests on them. I’ll keep each idea brief and point you to the Mini Encyclopedia at the back of the book whenever you want more depth.

Why KiCad splits the work in two

The first thing to notice, looking back, is that you used two separate programs to make one board. You drew the circuit in the Schematic Editor, saved it, and then moved to the PCB Editor to lay out the physical board. Two editors, two files, two distinct jobs.

If you’ve come from another design tool, or from drawing circuits by hand, this split is the single thing most likely to trip you up at the start, so it’s worth saying plainly why it’s there.

The schematic and the layout answer two completely different questions:

  • The schematic answers what connects to what. It’s a diagram of your circuit’s intent. It does not care where anything physically sits, how big the board is, or which way a chip faces. Move a symbol across the sheet and you’ve changed nothing electrically; you’ve just tidied the drawing.
  • The layout answers where everything physically goes. It’s the real board: copper of a real width, parts in real positions, holes of a real size. Here, where things sit matters enormously.

KiCad keeps these two worlds in separate editors on purpose, and it connects them with a one-way flow you already used without me drawing attention to it: you design the schematic first, then tell the PCB Editor to “Update PCB from Schematic” (Figure 4.1). The schematic is the source of truth. The layout follows it. You don’t draw your connections twice; you declare them once, in the schematic, and the layout inherits them.

This is where newcomers stumble. People expect one drawing surface where moving a part also moves its wiring, or where dragging a copper track changes the circuit. KiCad doesn’t work that way, and once you stop expecting it to, the tool gets faster, not slower. The separation buys you real advantages:

  • One schematic can become many different layouts. The same circuit might be a small round board today and a long thin one tomorrow, with no change to the underlying design.
  • KiCad can check each world on its own terms (more on that below), catching logical mistakes in the schematic and physical mistakes in the layout separately.
  • Your design intent never gets lost in the physical details. The schematic stays a clean, readable record of what the circuit is.

Other PCB packages make different choices, and some blur the line more than KiCad does. KiCad’s clean split is deliberate, and it’s the same split nearly every professional tool settles on once a design gets serious. Treat the schematic as the truth and the layout as its physical realisation, and the rest of this book will feel natural.

Two faces of a part, and a likeness

That two-editor split is also why every component in your design wore two faces, and why you had to deal with each one separately.

In the schematic you placed a ‘symbol.’ A symbol stands for a component’s function: a resistor’s symbol is the same zig-zag (or rectangle) no matter how big the actual resistor is. In the layout you placed a ‘footprint’: the real pattern of pads, holes, and silkscreen outline that the physical part solders onto. Remember the step in the layout tour where each part already knew its footprint? That’s because, back in the schematic, every symbol was paired with one.

Here’s the idea worth holding onto: one symbol can be paired with many different footprints. A resistor is a resistor in the circuit, but on the board it might be a tiny surface-mount chip, a larger one, or a through-hole part with its leads bent to fit. The symbol (the function) doesn’t change; the footprint (the physical reality) is your choice. Keeping the two separate is what lets you make that choice freely, and it’s the same separation you met in the previous section, now applied to individual parts.

It helps, then, to think of a part in KiCad not as a single object but as a small family. There is the symbol, its face on the schematic, standing for what the part does. There is the footprint, its face on the board, the physical pattern of pads and holes the real part solders onto. And there is usually a third member you have already seen without being told its name: the ‘3D model,’ the little solid shape that stood in for each part when you opened the 3D Viewer at the end of the layout tour. The 3D model rides along with the footprint and carries no electrical meaning at all; it is there purely so you can view the finished board as a physical object and catch a part facing the wrong way, or crowding its neighbour, before you spend a cent. Symbol, footprint, and model travel together as one part (Figure 4.2), and when this book says “find a part,” it means find the whole family.

Figure 4.2: One part (resistor R1), three faces. The symbol (1) is its function on the schematic, the footprint (2) its physical pattern on the board, and the 3D model (3) its likeness in the 3D viewer. One symbol can map to several footprints.

Where do all these symbols and footprints come from? They live in collections called ‘libraries,’ and KiCad ships with a large ‘standard library’ of them: thousands of symbols and footprints covering the parts you reach for most. Every part the blinker needed happened to live in that standard library, which is why neither tour had to leave the program to hunt one down. That convenience will carry you a long way, but it will not hold forever; sooner or later you will reach for a part the standard library does not have. When that day comes you are not stuck, because there is a clear, repeatable path to follow, and finding, importing, and keeping track of parts, right down to the 3D model that lets a new part appear in the 3D Viewer, is the whole subject of Chapter 6.

Nets: how connectivity crosses from schematic to board

So if the schematic holds the truth about what connects to what, how exactly does that truth reach the layout? The answer is a small, central idea called the ‘net.’

A net is simply one electrical connection: a single point that several pins all share. When you drew the wire in the schematic from the 555’s output to the resistor feeding the LED, the one you named BLINK, you didn’t really draw a wire, you declared that those pins belong to the same net. Every pin on that net is electrically the same point, no matter how the board eventually routes them.

When you updated the PCB from the schematic, KiCad carried every one of those nets across and showed them in the layout as thin straight lines connecting the pins that belong together. That tangle of lines is called the ‘ratsnest.’ It isn’t copper; it’s a to-do list. Each ratsnest line is KiCad saying, “these two pins need to be connected, but I haven’t been told how yet.” Routing, the work you did with the track tool, is nothing more than replacing each ratsnest line with real copper that honours the same connection (Figure 4.3). When the last ratsnest line disappears, every net the schematic declared has become a real path on the board.

This is the payoff of treating the schematic as the source of truth. You never tell the layout what connects to what; the nets already know, and the ratsnest just reminds you what’s left to draw.

Figure 4.3: A ratsnest line is a connection waiting to be routed. Routing turns it into copper.

Why KiCad checks your work twice

You ran two different checks while building the board, one in each editor, and now you can see why there were two.

In the schematic you ran the ‘Electrical Rules Check,’ or ERC. It studies the circuit’s logic: a power pin with nothing driving it, two outputs wired together, a pin left floating. These are mistakes of intent, so they belong in the editor that owns intent.

In the layout you ran the ‘Design Rules Check,’ or DRC. It studies the physical board against the rules of what can actually be manufactured: two tracks closer than the fab allows, a track touching a pad it shouldn’t, a hole too near the board edge. These are mistakes of physical reality, so they belong in the editor that owns physical reality.

Notice what the two checks have in common, because it marks the edge of what they can do for you. Each one tests your work against a body of rules: the ERC against the rules of electrical logic, the DRC against the rules of what a factory can build. Neither one knows what your circuit is supposed to do. A board can pass both checks without a single complaint and still be the wrong design, wired to do something other than what you intended, because breaking no rule is not the same as being correct. Deciding that the design itself is right stays your job, with the part’s datasheet as your authority. So read each check for what it is: a tireless catcher of rule-breaks, and now and then a tap on the shoulder asking you to confirm that you meant what you drew. Both projects later in the book stop on exactly this point, in a section called “Finding and Fixing a Design Defect,” where you will learn to tell a genuine mistake apart from the checker simply asking you to vouch for your intent.

The reason to run both, early and often, is money and time. A mistake caught in the ERC costs you a few seconds to fix. The same mistake discovered after you’ve paid a factory and waited two weeks for boards costs you the whole order. The two checks are KiCad’s way of letting you find problems while they’re still free to fix, and matching the two checks to the two editors is the same schematic-versus-layout split showing up one more time.

From your files to a board in your hand

Finally, a few terms surfaced in passing during the tour that are worth pinning down now. One belongs with the DRC you just met: while placing parts I mentioned a ‘keep-out area,’ a region where you tell KiCad not to place copper or parts, perhaps to leave room for a mounting screw or a tall connector. We didn’t need one on a board this simple, but it is how you write a physical constraint directly into the layout for the DRC to enforce.

The rest belong less to KiCad and more to the factory that builds your board. You met the board’s physical anatomy back in the first chapter, the layers, the copper, the soldermask and silkscreen, so I won’t repeat it here; the Mini Encyclopedia has each of those terms if you want a refresher. But the tour also produced a few features that are really about manufacturing, and they’re worth naming now that you’ve seen them.

When your board reaches a factory, it rarely travels alone. Fabs build many small boards together on one large sheet, called a ‘panel,’ and separate them afterward, which is why fixed-size, low-cost board services are so cheap: your handful of boards shares a panel with everyone else’s. You don’t have to think about panels for your first orders, but the word will come up the moment you read a fab’s website.

And if you ever have surface-mount parts placed by machine rather than by hand, three more terms appear together. The factory lays down ‘solder paste,’ a grey mixture of tiny solder beads and flux, onto the pads through a thin metal ‘paste stencil’ that has an opening over each pad. A ‘pick-and-place’ machine then sets each component onto its paste, and a pass through an oven melts the paste into finished solder joints. That’s the assembly service modern fabs offer, and it’s why surface-mount parts are no longer something to fear.

The bigger picture is how easy all of this has become. A modern fab will build your two-layer board in the colour and surface finish of your choice, for the price of a coffee or two, and will increasingly assemble it for you as well, fabrication, stencil, and pick-and-place under one roof. The factory side of PCB design has quietly turned into a commodity. That leaves the interesting part, the design, to you and to KiCad.

You now understand not just what you did in the last two chapters but why it works the way it does: one circuit, two editors, parts with two working faces and a likeness, connections carried by nets, and two checks guarding the path to a board you can hold.

KiCad Like a Pro, 5th Edition — Volume 1

Essential Recipes (Beginner)

You have now taken two boards all the way from a blank sheet to a parcel in the post, and along the way you learned a complete workflow twice over. This chapter is different from the ones before it. It is not a story with a beginning and an end; it is a drawer of small, useful tools. Each section is a self-contained ‘recipe’ for one task that did not belong to either project but that you will reach for again and again: putting your name on a board, rounding off its corners, measuring a gap, recovering yesterday’s work after a wrong move. You do not need to read them in order, and you certainly do not need to read them all at once. Skim the headings, and when a job comes up, come back and follow the steps. I have kept every recipe short and aimed it at the boards you already know, so nothing here should feel unfamiliar. Let’s open the drawer.

Silkscreen Text

You added a line of silkscreen text back in the layout tour, and you labelled the header pins on both projects so nobody could plug them in backwards. That same tool does a great deal more, and a few minutes spent here will make every board you build clearer to read and nicer to look at.

Text usually lives on the silkscreen, the printed layer on top of the soldermask, though you can place it on other layers too. To add a line of text, pick the layer you want first in the “Appearance” panel (the front silkscreen is F.Silkscreen), choose the text tool from the right toolbar, click where the text should sit, and type. While the text is selected you can open its “Text Properties” to set the size, thickness, and justification (Figure 11.1). In my example I have set the width and height to 1 mm and given the board the line “BB-PSU v1.0” on F.Silkscreen. The reason to choose a sensible size now is legibility: silkscreen has a smallest line the factory can print cleanly, and very small text turns to mud, so keep ordinary text around 1 mm tall and you will always be able to read it on the finished board.

Figure 11.1: Adding and styling silkscreen text. The “Text Properties” dialog sets the layer (F.Silkscreen), the width, the height, and the thickness. Keep ordinary text around 1 mm tall so the factory can print it cleanly.

Silkscreen is not just for words, though. The same layer will hold shapes, and even a logo, which is the next recipe.

Shapes, Logos, and Graphics

A board that explains itself, with a box around a section or your own mark in the corner, is a board you will be glad to hand to someone else. The silkscreen carries both, and neither costs you anything at the factory, so this is one of the cheapest ways to make your work look finished and read clearly.

Graphic shapes work much like text. The right toolbar has tools for lines, arcs, rectangles, circles, and polygons, and whatever you draw lands on the active layer, so choose F.Silkscreen in the “Appearance” panel first if you want the shape printed on the front of the board. A box drawn around a block of the circuit, a short line pointing at a connector, or a small arrow marking pin 1 helps whoever assembles the board, which is often you, six months from now. These are ordinary drawing objects: select one and its properties let you set the line width and, for a closed shape, whether the inside is filled. Keep the lines a touch above the factory’s silkscreen minimum, around 0.15 mm, so thin strokes do not drop out in printing.

The most satisfying trick is putting a logo on a board. KiCad cannot drop an ordinary picture file straight onto the silkscreen, so it gives you a small companion app, the ‘Image Converter,’ which turns a black-and-white image into a footprint the board editor understands. Launch it from the KiCad project manager (or with “Ctrl+B”) and open your image. Three tabs across the top, “Original Picture,” “Greyscale Picture,” and “Black & White Picture,” let you watch your image reduce to the pure black and white that silkscreen actually prints; nudge the “Black / white threshold” slider until the shape reads cleanly. Then set the output format to “Footprint (.kicad_mod file),” choose F.Silkscreen as the layer, and click “Export to File…” (Figure 11.2). Back in the PCB Editor you place that footprint like any other part, and your logo rides along on the board. Keep the image simple and high in contrast; fine detail and soft shades of grey do not survive the trip to bare silkscreen.

Figure 11.2: The Image Converter turns a simple black-and-white logo (here a gear) into a footprint you can place on the silkscreen. The output format is set to “Footprint,” on the F.Silkscreen layer.

Reshape the Board Outline

Every board you have drawn so far has been a rectangle, because a rectangle is the right answer most of the time. But the outline is just a drawing on one special layer, the Edge.Cuts layer, and the factory will cut along whatever shape you put there. The one rule that never bends is this: the outline must be a single, closed loop. Leave a gap anywhere, or let two shapes overlap, and KiCad can no longer tell what is inside the board and what is outside, so the 3D viewer and some checks quietly stop working until you fix it.

The reshaping you will want most often is to round off the sharp corners, so the board slips into a rounded case or simply feels nicer in the hand. You met this back in the layout tour: you do not redraw anything, you just soften the corners you already have. Select the whole outline, right-click, and choose “Shape Modification,” then “Fillet Lines…” (❶ and ❷ in Figure 11.3). KiCad asks for a radius, and a ‘fillet’ (a rounded corner, as opposed to a sharp one) of 2 mm gives a tidy result on a board this size (❸). Click “OK” and the corners become gentle curves. The same submenu offers “Chamfer Lines…,” which cuts a straight bevel instead of a curve, and “Dogbone Corners…,” a specialist option for corners that will be milled; fillet is the one you will reach for most.

Figure 11.3: Rounding the corners with “Shape Modification → Fillet Lines…”. Select the outline (❶), choose “Fillet Lines…” (❷), and enter a radius, here 2 mm (❸). The sharp corner on the left becomes the rounded one on the right.

That tool reshapes an outline you already have. If you ever need a shape a rectangle cannot give you, you can also build an outline from scratch: delete the rectangle and draw the edges yourself on Edge.Cuts, a straight line along each side and a small arc at each corner, meeting end to end so the whole shape closes, or click out a free form with the polygon tool. This still works, and it is how earlier versions of KiCad made rounded corners, but it is fiddly and slow next to the fillet tool, so save it for the odd shape that really needs it. Whatever you draw, finish by running the design rules check, which complains at once if the loop is not closed, and glance at the 3D viewer to confirm the board is the shape you meant.

Flip a Part to the Back

On both projects you placed every component on the top of the board, which is the simplest way to work and perfectly fine for a beginner. Sooner or later, though, you will want a part on the underside: a connector that has to face the other way, or a few small parts tucked onto the back to keep the board small. Moving a component to the other side is a single keystroke. Select the footprint and press “F” for flip. The part jumps to the bottom of the board, and everything that belongs to it follows: its copper pads move from the front copper to the back, and its silkscreen outline moves to the back silkscreen. If you would rather be explicit, or you are already adjusting a part in its “Footprint Properties,” you can change the “Side” field from “Front” to “Back” there instead, which does exactly the same thing (❶ in Figure 11.4). The board redraws with the part on the underside (❷).

Figure 11.4: Flipping a part to the underside. Pressing “F” does it in one keystroke; the “Footprint Properties” dialog does the same by setting “Side” to “Back” (❶) and clicking “OK” (❷). On the right, the connector now sits on the back of the board.

That mirroring is the thing to keep in mind. A part on the back is drawn as if you were looking through the board from the top, so its pads and text appear reversed on screen. That is correct, not a mistake, and it is exactly how the part will sit when the board is in your hand. As always, the 3D viewer is the quickest way to confirm a flipped part landed where you intended: turn the board over, and there it is.

Measure a Distance

Again and again while laying out a board you will want to know a distance. Will this connector clear that capacitor? Is the mounting hole far enough from the edge? How wide is the gap I just left between two tracks? KiCad has a ‘measuring tool’ for exactly these questions, and it changes nothing on the board, so you can reach for it freely. Start it from the right toolbar (❶ in Figure 11.5) or with “Ctrl+Shift+M”, click once where you want to measure from (❷), and move the cursor. A live readout follows your pointer (❸) showing the straight-line distance, the separate horizontal and vertical distances, and the angle. Click again to drop the measurement, or press “Esc” to put the tool away.

Figure 11.5: The measuring tool (❶) reports the distance from a start point (❷) to the cursor, with the live readout (❸) showing the total distance, the X and Y distances, and the angle. It changes nothing on the board.

It is a small tool, but it settles the questions that otherwise nag at you. When you are not sure two parts will sit side by side, measure the gap rather than guess, and if the number worries you, move one of them now, while the board is still on the screen and not yet at the factory. One thing to know is that this measurement is fleeting: the moment you click elsewhere or leave the tool, it disappears. It is meant for a quick check, not for a number you want to keep. When you do want a measurement that stays on the board, reach for a dimension instead, which is the next recipe.

Add a Permanent Dimension

A dimension is a measurement that lives on the board as a real drawing object: a line with arrowheads and a number, the kind you see on any engineering drawing. Unlike the measuring tool, it stays where you put it, saved with the board and shown every time you open it. That makes it the right choice when the number matters to someone beyond this moment: the height of the board, the spacing of two mounting holes, the pitch of a connector, anything a future you, a fabricator, or a reader of your documentation needs to see at a glance.

The one you will use most is the ‘orthogonal dimension,’ which measures a purely horizontal or vertical distance. Before you draw it, pick the layer it should live on, because a dimension is documentation, not copper or silkscreen, and it does not belong on a fabricated layer. In my example I chose User.1 in the “Appearance” panel, selected the orthogonal dimension tool, and clicked the top edge of the breadboard supply and then the bottom edge; KiCad drew the “37.8” height you see down the right-hand side (Figure 11.6). The dimension is now part of the board. Move a part or drag an edge and it stays put, ready to remind you how tall the board is.

Figure 11.6: An orthogonal dimension recording the board’s height, 37.8 mm, drawn on the User.1 layer (selected in the “Appearance” panel on the right). Unlike a quick measurement, it is saved with the board.

That choice of User.1 is worth a word, because KiCad gives you several layers that never reach the factory as copper or silkscreen and exist purely for your own notes. The four numbered ones, User.1 through User.4, are blank general-purpose layers you can use however you like: dimensions on one, assembly notes on another, a mechanical outline on a third. Alongside them sit a few named layers with the same spirit, User.Drawings and User.Comments for documentation, and User.Eco1 and User.Eco2, whose names come from ‘engineering change order’ and which people use as scratch layers. None of these is manufactured, so you can annotate freely without adding a thing to the finished board; just keep your dimensions off Edge.Cuts and the silkscreen, where they would confuse the cut or print for real.

The orthogonal tool is one of a small family. Long-click the dimension button on the right toolbar and a flyout opens with the rest of them (Figure 11.7). Alongside orthogonal there is the ‘aligned’ dimension, which measures along a slanted line rather than snapping to horizontal or vertical; the ‘center’ dimension, which marks the distance between two points as a centreline; the ‘radial’ dimension, for the radius of an arc or a hole; and the ‘leader,’ an arrow with a text note for pointing at a feature and labelling it. You will not need all of them on a simple board, but it is good to know the drawer is there when a design calls for it.

Figure 11.7: Long-clicking the dimension button opens the full family of dimension tools: aligned, orthogonal, center, radial, and the leader note.

Grid Overrides

The grid is the invisible net your cursor snaps to, and a well-chosen grid is what keeps a layout tidy: parts line up, tracks meet pads cleanly, and nothing sits a hair off where you meant it. You met the grid early on and switched it by hand a few times, a coarse grid for placing parts, a finer one for nudging a track. KiCad can do that switching for you. The feature is called ‘grid overrides’, and it lets you set a different grid for different kinds of object, so the editor uses a coarse grid while you move footprints and a finer one the moment you start routing a track.

You set this up once in the Preferences dialog, under the PCB Editor’s grid settings, where you can give footprints and pads, tracks, vias, text, and graphics each their own grid and switch any of those overrides on or off (Figure 11.8). After that you do not have to think about it again; the right grid simply arrives when you need it. If you ever want to ignore the overrides for a moment and work on one plain grid, the button on the left toolbar, or the “Ctrl+Shift+G” shortcut, turns all the overrides off and back on again.

Figure 11.8: Grid overrides assign a separate grid to each kind of object, footprints and pads, tracks, vias, text, and graphics, so KiCad switches grids for you as you work.

Board-Setup Basics

You opened the Board Setup dialog once, on the solar project, to look at the design rules and then wisely leave them alone. It is worth knowing what else lives in there, because it is the one place where a board’s ground rules are set. Open it from the “File” menu with “Board Setup…”, or from the matching button on the top toolbar (Figure 11.9).

Figure 11.9: The Board Setup dialog, open on the “Constraints” page. For a first board, confirm the default constraints and, if you like, add a couple of predefined track widths.

Three sections cover almost everything a beginner needs. The “Constraints” section holds the minimum trace width, clearance, and hole sizes for the whole board, the very limits we talked about in the manufacturing chapter. The safe move for now is the one you already made: leave them at the defaults, which sit well inside what any ordinary factory can build. The “Pre-defined Sizes” section is the more immediately handy one. Back on the breadboard supply you set a wider track by hand every time the main power path needed one. Instead, list the widths you expect to use here, say 0.4 mm for signals and 0.8 mm for power, and while routing you can step through that list with a keypress rather than retyping the number each time. The third section worth a glance is “Net Classes”. A ‘net class’ is a named group of nets that share the same track width and clearance, so you can say “all power nets are 0.8 mm wide” once, rather than setting every track by hand. You do not need net classes for the simple boards in this volume, but it is good to know where that lever is for the day you want it.

The thing to take away is that Board Setup is where you describe the board to itself, once, before you route. For Volume 1 you touch it lightly: confirm the defaults, perhaps add a couple of predefined track widths, and move on.

Highlight a Net (Cross-Probe)

When a board gets busy, it is easy to lose track of where a particular signal goes. KiCad gives you two quick ways to see a connection light up. The first is net highlighting. Hover the cursor over any pad or track and press the backtick key (`), and every piece of copper on that same net, the whole electrical connection, brightens while everything else dims (Figure 11.10). It is the fastest way to answer “where does this actually connect?” To clear the highlight, press the backtick again over an empty area, or press “Esc”.

Figure 11.10: Highlighting a net with the backtick key in the PCB Editor. The tracks on the chosen net (the arrow points to one) stay bright while the rest of the board dims.

The same key works in the Schematic Editor. Hover over a wire and press the backtick, and that net lights up on the schematic just as it does on the board. Better still, the two editors talk to each other. Open the schematic and the board side by side, highlight a net on the schematic, and the matching copper brightens on the board at the same time (Figure 11.11). This is called ‘cross-probing’, and net highlighting is only part of it.

Figure 11.11: Cross-probing a net. Pressing the backtick over a net in the Schematic Editor (below) highlights the same net in the PCB Editor (above), so you can see where a wire on the circuit becomes copper on the board.

Cross-probing works for components as well as nets. Click a part or a pin in the schematic and KiCad selects the matching footprint on the board, panning the view so the part is in front of you (Figure 11.12); click a footprint on the board and it jumps back to the symbol on the schematic. Remember that the schematic is your source of truth, so this is how you walk between the circuit you drew and the board you are building, checking that the thing you wired is the thing you routed. It works with the 3D viewer too, where clicking a part selects it on the board, which is a lovely way to track down a component you can see but cannot name.

Figure 11.12: Component cross-probing. Clicking symbol U80 in the Schematic Editor (❶) selects and pans to its matching footprint in the PCB Editor (❷).

If cross-probing ever does more or less than you want, for instance if you would rather it did not pan the view, you can tune its behaviour in “Preferences → Display Options,” which both the Schematic Editor and the PCB Editor have. As an aside, the busy board in these three screenshots is not one of ours; it is one of the example projects that ship with KiCad, the NVIDIA Jetson AGX Thor baseboard, which appears to have been designed in KiCad itself. It makes a good stand-in for the kind of dense, many-net board where highlighting and cross-probing earn their keep.

Save a 3D Snapshot

The 3D viewer has been your sanity check throughout this book, the place you spin a board around to make sure nothing fouls anything else. It is also the place to make a picture of your work worth sharing. Open it from the “View” menu or with “Alt+3”. Drag with the left mouse button to orbit the board, roll the wheel to zoom, and drag with the middle button to pan; if you lose your bearings, the little navigation widget of standard views (front, back, top, and so on) snaps you back to a clean angle. When you have framed a view you like, save it to a file with “Export Image…” in the “File” menu (❶ in Figure 11.13), where you can choose the size and resolution (❷), or send it straight to the clipboard to paste into a message.

Figure 11.13: Saving a snapshot from the 3D viewer. “File → Export Image…” (❶) opens the “Export 3D View” dialog, where you set the image size and resolution before clicking “OK” (❷).

For a genuinely handsome render, turn on ‘raytracing’ first, from the toolbar button at the top of the viewer or in its “Preferences” (❶ in Figure 11.14). Raytracing lights and shadows the board far more realistically than the standard view, at the cost of taking a little longer to draw. It is the difference between a snapshot and a portrait, and it is how those glossy board pictures you have seen online are made. None of this changes your design; it is purely a way to show it off, and, more usefully, to send someone a clear picture of a board before it exists.

Figure 11.14: The same board with raytracing switched on (❶). The lighting, shadows, and soft reflections turn a working view into a picture worth sharing.

Export a Schematic PDF

A schematic is meant to be read, and not always on the screen where you drew it. When you want to email the circuit to someone, print it to mark up on paper, or simply keep a tidy copy alongside your notes, export it as a PDF. In the Schematic Editor, open the “File” menu and choose “Plot…”, or click the plot button on the top toolbar (❶ in Figure 11.15). Set the output format to “PDF” (❷), decide whether to include the drawing sheet (the border and title block around the schematic, usually worth keeping), and choose colour or black and white. Click “Plot All Pages” (❸) and KiCad writes the PDF, gathering a multi-sheet design into a single file with one page per sheet.

Figure 11.15: Exporting the schematic to PDF from the “Plot Schematic” dialog (opened with ❶). Set the format to “PDF” (❷), keep “Plot drawing sheet” ticked for a titled, bordered copy, and click “Plot All Pages” (❸).

This is the schematic counterpart of the Gerbers you plot from the board: the same idea of producing a clean file to hand off, except this file is for people to read rather than for a factory to build. If all you want is a paper copy, “Print…” in the same menu sends the schematic straight to a printer. Either way you end up with a portable, readable record of the circuit that anyone can open without KiCad installed.

Recover an Earlier Version

Everyone, sooner or later, makes a change they wish they could take back: a part deleted, a careful arrangement nudged into chaos, an hour of routing undone by a stray keystroke. Undo handles the small slips, but for the larger “I want yesterday’s version back” moments, KiCad keeps a safety net called ‘Local History’. As you work, it saves snapshots of your project behind the scenes, so the recent past is always recoverable.

There is one catch that trips people up, and it tripped me: Local History does nothing until you turn on the backups that feed it, so on a fresh install the panel is empty. It is not a GitHub feature and it needs no account; it rides on KiCad’s own project backups. To switch it on, open “Preferences → Common” and find the “Project backup” section. Tick “Automatically backup projects,” then set the “Format” to “Incremental” rather than “Zip archive”. The incremental format is the one that keeps the running snapshots Local History reads; the zip format simply writes whole-project archives and leaves the panel empty. Behind the scenes KiCad manages a hidden .history folder for you as an ordinary Git repository, so there is nothing else to set up and no commands to type.

With that in place, open the “Local History” panel from the “View” menu, under “Panels” (Figure 11.16). You will see a list of snapshots, each with the time it was taken; hover over one and KiCad shows you which files it would bring back. When you find the moment you want to return to, right-click that snapshot and choose “Restore Commit”, and your project is rolled back to how it was then.

Figure 11.16: The Local History panel keeps timed snapshots of your project. Right-click one and choose “Restore Commit” to roll back.

I mention this not because you will need it often, but because knowing it is there changes how it feels to experiment. You can try a bold rearrangement, or tear up half a layout to route it a better way, without the small fear that you are gambling with work you cannot get back. The net is under you. That said, Local History is a convenience, not a backup plan for your life’s work: keep your important projects somewhere safe of their own, and treat this as the fast way to undo a bad afternoon.

Customise Your Hotkeys

By now you have a feel for the keys you press most: “X” to route, “M” to move, “R” to rotate. KiCad lets you change any of them, and add shortcuts to the many commands that have none by default, so the editor fits your hands rather than the other way around. Open the Preferences dialog (the “Preferences” menu, or “Ctrl+,”) and choose the “Hotkeys” page (Figure 11.17). You are looking at the full list of commands and the key, if any, assigned to each. To set or change one, double-click the command and press the combination you want. If that combination is already in use, KiCad tells you and offers to move it, so you cannot create a silent conflict.

Figure 11.17: The “Hotkeys” page of the Preferences dialog. This is the editable list: double-click a command to assign the key combination that suits you.

One thing catches almost every newcomer, and it caught me. There are two hotkey lists in KiCad, and only one of them lets you make changes. The page just described, in “Preferences → Hotkeys”, is the editable one. The other, reached from “Help → List Hotkeys”, opens a window titled “Hotkey List” that looks similar but is only a reference: it shows every current binding at a glance, which is handy, but double-clicking a row there does nothing (Figure 11.18). If you have ever tried to rebind a key and found it would not take, this is almost certainly why. Make your changes in Preferences; use the Help list only to look things up.

Figure 11.18: The “Hotkey List” from “Help → List Hotkeys” is a read-only reference. It looks like the editable page but is only for looking bindings up, not changing them.

A few things make this safe to play with. Commands you have changed are marked with a star until you save, you can undo a single change or reset everything to the defaults from the buttons below the list, and the common shortcuts are shared across all of KiCad’s editors, so a key you teach yourself in the board editor means the same thing in the schematic. My advice for a beginner is a light touch: live with the defaults for a while, notice the one or two moves that feel awkward under your fingers, and rebind only those. The defaults were chosen with care, and the aim is to smooth a couple of rough edges, not to rebuild the whole keyboard.

Plugin and Content Manager

KiCad does a great deal on its own, but it also opens its doors to work shared by its community, and the front door is the ‘Plugin and Content Manager’, or PCM. Launch it from the main KiCad project manager window (Figure 11.19). Inside you will find three kinds of add-on, each on its own tab. Plugins add new tools and commands, often automating a fiddly job or adding a feature for a particular task, including helpers that some manufacturers offer for ordering boards. Libraries add ready-made symbols and footprints to draw from, on top of the generous set KiCad already ships with. Color themes simply restyle the editors, which is a small pleasure but a real one if a darker canvas is easier on your eyes.

Figure 11.19: The Plugin and Content Manager. Browse the repository (❶) across its tabs for plugins, libraries, and color themes, read what a package does, and install it with a click (❸ or ❹).

Installing is the same gentle loop for all three: browse or search the list, read what a package does, and click to install; KiCad downloads it and wires it in. A library you add this way is registered for you, so it appears in the choosers without your editing any tables by hand. Updates and removals live in the same place, so nothing you add here is permanent. One word of sense, though: everything in the manager is contributed by other people, so install what you have a use for and recognise, rather than collecting add-ons for their own sake. A handful of well-chosen plugins can save you real time; an editor cluttered with tools you never touch just gets in the way.

That is the drawer of recipes, and it will grow on you. None of these is essential to finishing a board, which is why they sat outside the two projects, but each one smooths a corner of the work, and together they are the difference between fighting the tool and flowing with it. Keep this chapter in mind less as something you have read and more as something to come back to.

That also brings Volume 1 to a close. You started not knowing what a PCB was, and you end having designed, checked, ordered, and understood two of them, with a handful of recipes in your pocket besides. The last chapter is not one to read straight through at all: it is a ‘mini-encyclopedia’, an illustrated glossary of every term this book has introduced, gathered in one place so you can look anything up the moment a word stops you. Turn to it whenever you need it. And congratulations: you are a beginner no longer.
